# A Real-World Primer: What an Investment Firm Actually Does

A standing reference companion, written for someone who knows little about finance, so the
fiction can borrow the *shape* of the real thing. The story's setting is an unnamed country,
so treat jurisdiction-specific details (filing names, fee percentages) as **flavor and
ballpark**, not gospel — they vary by country. The point is the *logic*: who manages whose
money, how they get paid, and what the year actually feels like.

For how this maps onto Turner & Associates specifically — what works, what to change — see
`company-audit.md`. This file is just the real-world groundwork.

---

## 1. The first thing to get straight: whose money is it?

Almost every confusion about "finance companies" dissolves once you ask one question: **does
the firm manage its *own* money/deals, or does it manage *other people's* money for a fee?**

| Type | What they do | How they earn | Closest to T&A? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Asset / investment management** | Invest *clients'* money in markets (stocks, bonds, etc.) on their behalf | A **management fee** = a small % of the money they manage, every year | **YES — this is the model** |
| **Wealth management / private banking** | Same, but for *rich individuals/families* — plus tax, estate, planning | % of assets, sometimes planning fees | Partial overlap |
| **Family office / multi-family office** | Manage one or a few enormous family fortunes, soup to nuts | Retainer or % of assets | Partial overlap |
| **OCIO (outsourced chief investment office)** | Be the *entire* investment department for an institution (a foundation, a trust, a pension) | % of assets | Strong overlap |
| **Hedge fund** | Aggressive pooled investing, big bets, wealthy/institutional clients | "2 and 20": ~2% fee + ~20% of profits | Not really |
| **Private equity / venture capital** | *Buy* whole companies or stakes, hold for years, sell at a profit | Fee + share of profits ("carry") | No |
| **Investment bank** | *Advise* on deals: mergers, IPOs, raising money. Does **not** manage portfolios | Deal fees, commissions | No (different world) |
| **Brokerage / sell-side** | Execute trades, publish research to *sell* to investors | Commissions | No |

**Turner & Associates is a boutique investment management firm** — it invests money on behalf
of a handful of large clients (institutions, trusts, old-money families, companies) and is
paid a fee for doing it well. The clients' money is held in **portfolios**; the firm decides
how it's invested and reports back. That single fact explains the "portfolios," "allocations,"
"quarterly reports," "risk assessments," and "client reviews" all over the manuscript.

> **The crucial distinction for your story:** a **CFO** runs *the firm's own* finances —
> payroll, budgets, the company's books. The people who manage *clients'* portfolios are the
> **CIO (Chief Investment Officer), portfolio managers, and investment analysts.** These are two
> different jobs — and Ian holds **both** (a combined CFO + CIO role, normal at a founder-led
> boutique). That's why he runs the clockwork accounting *and* the marquee portfolios. (See
> `company-audit.md` §2.)

## 2. Who's who inside the firm (the org chart)

Investment firms split into **front / middle / back office** — a standard piece of jargon:

- **Front office** (makes the money / faces the client):
  - **CIO / Head of Investments** — owns the investment strategy; the buck stops here on *how
    client money is invested*. **This is Ian's job — one of his two hats** (see CFO below).
  - **Portfolio Managers (PMs)** — run specific portfolios; make the buy/sell calls. Senior
    analysts like **Amy and Max** read as PMs or senior investment analysts.
  - **Investment / research analysts (buy-side)** — dig into companies, sectors, and data to
    feed the PMs. **Luc**, and later **Rin/Gal**, are juniors here.
  - **Client / relationship managers, business development** — win and keep clients. **Leo**
    (charming, "handles client relations," lands the big fish) is **Head of Business Development /
    Client Relations** and a partner/heir — *not* the CEO (a `characters.md` mislabel; see
    `company-audit.md` §3). **Ela** ("client communications") is relationship management.
- **Middle office** (controls the risk):
  - **Risk** — measures how much danger is in the portfolios. **Zoe** ("portfolio risk
    assessments").
  - **Compliance** — keeps the firm on the right side of regulators (the "compliance audit"
    Jay does for Stormfront lives here).
- **Back / "middle" support** (keeps it running):
  - **Operations** — settles trades, produces the client statements/reports.
  - **Data / quant / technology** — builds the models and the systems. **Kim** ("technical
    specialist, database management") — and note the *dropped table* in Book 2 is a real,
    plausible disaster in this seat.
- **Corporate** (runs the company itself):
  - **CEO / Managing Partner** — runs the business. At T&A this is effectively **Turner Senior**
    (founder/chairman, still the active top authority) — *not* Leo. There's no separate modern
    "CEO"; Ian is the day-to-day operator (see `company-audit.md` §3).
  - **CFO** — runs the *firm's own* money (payroll, budgets, the books, the year-end bonus
    machine). **Ian's other hat** — he holds this *and* the CIO role (see `company-audit.md` §2).
  - **Founder / Chairman** — **Turner Senior.**

**Buy-side vs sell-side** (you'll hear this constantly): the *buy-side* manages money and
*buys* investments (that's T&A). The *sell-side* (banks, brokers) *sells* securities and
research to the buy-side. T&A is firmly buy-side.

**Fiduciary duty:** a manager investing clients' money is usually legally bound to act in the
*client's* best interest, not the firm's. This is a nice, true detail that fits Ian's ethical
rigidity — it's not just personality, it's the job's legal spine.

## 3. How the money actually works (so the lavishness can add up)

Revenue = **AUM × fee**.

- **AUM** = "Assets Under Management" = the total client money the firm invests. The single
  most important number at an asset manager.
- **Management fee** = a small annual percentage of AUM. Traditional managers charge anywhere
  from ~0.3% to ~1%+ per year (institutions pay less, specialized/boutique more). Often quoted
  in **basis points** ("bps"): 1 bp = 0.01%, so "50 bps" = 0.50%.
- Some also charge a **performance fee** — a slice of the profits above a benchmark — which is
  where the really big, lumpy money (and big bonuses) come from in good years.

**Does the economics of T&A close?** Roughly, yes — *if the clients are large.* Sketch:
- ~100 staff at a generous boutique, fully loaded (pay + perks + overhead), might cost on the
  order of tens of millions a year.
- To cover that comfortably and still be very profitable, you'd want revenue in the high tens
  of millions.
- At, say, 0.5–1% fees, that implies **several billion in AUM**, spread across the marquee
  clients — i.e., top clients worth **hundreds of millions to billions each.**

That's entirely realistic for a white-glove boutique serving institutions, trusts, and
old-money fortunes. So "≈20 marquee clients, 100 staff, absurd perks" **can** fund itself —
the trick is just that those clients are *huge*. (One tidy fix: make explicit that the <20
"crown-jewel" accounts are the *elite tier*, while the "three smaller teams" handle a longer
tail of ordinary clients — the manuscript already implies this.)

## 4. The calendar — the part you actually asked about

Real investment firms run on a genuine, recurring rhythm. Here's the true cycle, and where the
fiction's "quarterly reports" and "strategy season" do and don't line up.

### The four quarter-ends (Mar 31 · Jun 30 · Sep 30 · Dec 31)
After each quarter closes, there's a **2–4 week crunch**:
- **Client quarterly reporting** — every client gets a performance report/statement: how their
  portfolio did, vs. its benchmark, what changed, commentary. This is the real-world engine of
  the manuscript's "quarterly reports." ✅ The *shape* is exactly right.
- **Rebalancing** — nudging portfolios back to their target mix.
- **Regulatory filings** — e.g., large US managers file a "13F" (a list of holdings) within
  **45 days** of quarter-end. (US-specific; use as flavor.)

### Earnings season (≈ mid-Jan / mid-Apr / mid-Jul / mid-Oct)
Public companies report results four times a year, bunched into a few intense weeks. For
*research analysts* this is a real grind — a second, overlapping quarterly heartbeat. **This is
a gift for your story:** the team's quarterly crunch can plausibly be *both* "client reporting"
**and** "earnings season research" landing at once. It makes the four-times-a-year death march
feel earned and real.

### Year-end → Q1 (December–March): the genuinely brutal stretch
This is when everything compounds, and it's the real-life heaviest season — your instinct to
make Jan/Feb/Mar the worst is **correct**:
- **Annual client reviews** (the big once-a-year sit-downs).
- **Annual report + audited financials** — external auditors descend ("audit season").
- **Tax reporting** — year-end tax documents go out to clients in roughly Jan–Feb (US: 1099s,
  K-1s); messy and deadline-driven.
- **Annual regulatory filings** — e.g., US registered advisers update "Form ADV" within ~90
  days of fiscal year-end. (Flavor.)
- **Annual strategy / asset-allocation review** — the firm refreshes its market outlook and
  its "how should client money be allocated this year" plan. **This is the realistic seed of
  your "strategy season."**

### Bonus timing
- Comp is reviewed late in the year; **annual bonuses are typically paid Dec–Feb** (often
  Jan/Feb). ✅ Your January year-end bonus is dead-on.
- At senior/elite levels the bonus is the *main event* — it can be a large multiple of base
  salary, and it swings with the firm's year (hence "record year → mind-blowing bonus").
- **Monthly and quarterly *guaranteed* bonuses are not a standard finance structure** — see
  `company-audit.md` §4 for how to keep the generosity while making the *form* legible.

### Other real annual rhythms (optional texture)
- **IPS reviews** — each client has an "Investment Policy Statement" (the rulebook for their
  portfolio); these get reviewed annually.
- **Capital market assumptions** — the firm's annual refresh of "what we expect markets to
  do," which feeds the strategy review.
- **Regulatory exams** — periodically a regulator inspects the firm; stressful, real.

## 5. Glossary (quick reference)

- **AUM** — Assets Under Management; the client money the firm invests. The headline number.
- **Buy-side / sell-side** — managers who *buy/hold* investments (T&A) vs. banks/brokers who
  *sell* securities and research.
- **Front / middle / back office** — revenue roles / risk-and-control roles / operational
  support.
- **CIO** — Chief Investment Officer; owns investment strategy. *(One of Ian's two hats, with CFO.)*
- **Portfolio Manager (PM)** — runs a portfolio; makes the calls.
- **Mandate** — the specific job a client hires you for ("manage our pension's equities").
- **Portfolio** — a client's pool of investments.
- **Asset allocation** — how a portfolio is split across stocks/bonds/etc. Rebalancing =
  restoring the target split.
- **Benchmark** — the yardstick a portfolio is measured against (e.g., a market index).
- **IPS (Investment Policy Statement)** — the client's rulebook: goals, risk limits,
  constraints.
- **Due diligence** — deep investigation before trusting a counterparty/investment. *(Also the
  legitimate lever in the Cruver redesign — see `company-audit.md`.)*
- **Compliance** — staying within the rules/regulators.
- **Risk management** — measuring and limiting how much can go wrong.
- **Management fee / performance fee** — the % of AUM you charge / the cut of profits you take.
- **Basis point (bp)** — 0.01%. Fees and returns are quoted in bps.
- **Fiduciary** — legally bound to put the client's interest first.
- **Liquidity** — how easily an asset can be turned into cash.
- **Drawdown** — a peak-to-trough loss; how much a portfolio fell.
- **Counterparty** — the other firm on the far side of a deal/relationship (e.g., Orrey).
- **Earnings season** — the four annual windows when public companies report results.
- **Quarter-end / year-end** — the reporting/closing dates that drive the calendar.

## 6. Where the book idealizes (so you can do it on purpose)

The setting is a deliberately rosy "what if a finance boutique were a genuinely wonderful place
to work" — a low-key, insider version of the old "Google was a dream job" idea. That's a valid
fantasy; just know the three dials you've turned past real life, all defensible as *choices*:

1. **Comp cadence** — real comp is *high base + one big discretionary annual bonus*, not
   guaranteed monthly/quarterly bonuses. (Keep the generosity; relabel the form — `company-
   audit.md` §4.)
2. **Crunch intensity** — "living in the office in sleeping bags for a week" is *deal-team /
   audit-busy-season* brutality, heavier than a well-run asset-management boutique's quarterly
   reporting usually is. Fine as this firm's particular culture; even better if the later
   hiring of Ray/Liv visibly *tames* it (which it does).
3. **Perks** — car service, lavish events, premium everything: real at the top, dialed up here.
   Already established as deliberate.

None of these break the world. They just mean T&A is the **best-case, slightly-mythical**
version of a real model — which is exactly the intent.
