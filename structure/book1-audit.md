## 1. Headline findings (the things to fix first)

1. **[HIGH] Tim's age and birth order are self-contradictory and break a Book 3
   plot point.** This is the single worst character inconsistency in the book.
2. **[HIGH] The office floor changes from the 32nd to the 34th** partway through.
3. **[MED] Jay's employment history is told two different ways** (3 years / one
   firm vs. 6 years / three firms).
4. **[MED] The Orrey/Cruver takedown is the book's biggest plausibility outlier**
   — thriller-grade, and it makes Ian briefly omnipotent.
5. **[MED] The half-siblings' ages don't reconcile** even setting Tim aside (Ema
   especially), and `characters.md` is itself internally shaky here.

## 2. Character fidelity

Overall the two leads are rendered **consistently** with both `characters.md`
and the craft-notes intent. Ian reads as the man who "manages well, at a price";
Jay as "anxious and shows up anyway." Physical descriptions hold throughout
(Ian: tall, blonde, blue-eyed, athletic; Jay: gray eyes, pale skin, dark hair,
strikingly attractive). The supporting cast keep their established voices (Leo
the meddler, Max the tactless-but-warm, Luc the workplace friend, Amy the sharp
maternal anchor, Kim's adventurous palate, Zoe the quiet diver). The problems
are in the **family** and in a few **factual** details, not in the leads'
personalities.

### 2.1 Jay's half-siblings — the Tim problem **[HIGH]**

The manuscript can't decide who Tim is.

- **ch08 (Christmas gifts):** "For Tim, **the oldest at seventeen** and rarely
  indoors… a military grade multitool." Order given: Ema 15, **Tim 17**, Ari 12.
- **ch15 (Easter call):** "**Tim** was already **considering college
  applications**." → again the oldest.
- **ch19 (desk photo):** "Tim lurking behind him, already taller than Jay
  **despite being the younger brother**." → now the youngest.
- **`characters.md` / Book 3 plan:** Tim is the **youngest**, "turns 18 in Book
  3," and Book 3 hangs a whole beat on **Jay buying Tim a car for his 18th
  birthday**.

So in two chapters Tim is the *college-bound eldest*, and in a third (plus the
canon reference and a load-bearing Book 3 plot point) he's the *younger
brother*. Effectively the names **Tim and Ari are swapped** between the
Book 1 prose (Tim = oldest, Ari = youngest math kid) and the canon (Tim =
youngest, turns 18 in Book 3).

Because the Book 3 car gift depends on **Tim turning 18 in Book 3**, Tim must be
the youngest and ~15–16 in Book 1. **Recommendation:** reassign in ch08 and ch15
so the *oldest* (17, multitool/camping, college applications) is **Ari**, and
**Tim** is the youngest — matching ch19 and the reference. Then build a one-page
sibling bible (see 2.3).

### 2.2 Ema's age **[MED]**

- ch08, ch14: Ema is **15** and graduating **middle school** (ch14: "His
  half-sister was fifteen, which made middle school graduation fairly
  predictable"). Fifteen is old for a middle-school graduation in most systems.
- `characters.md`: Ema "**13–14** in Book 1."

Pick one. If she's graduating middle school, 13–14 is the natural age; 15 reads
as held-back. Either lower her to 14 in the prose or change "middle school" to
something that fits 15.

### 2.3 The sibling data can't currently be reconciled — build a bible **[MED]**

Even ignoring the Tim swap, the numbers fight each other:

- ch07 establishes birth order as: a stepbrother born when Jay was ~12 (so ~17
  now), then a daughter, then a youngest son "**sixteen years younger than
  Jay**" (~13–14).
- That makes the **eldest** half-sibling a **boy ~17** and the **youngest** a
  **boy ~13–14**, with Ema the girl in the middle.
- But `characters.md` simultaneously says Ema is 13–14 (youngest-ish), Ari is
  "early teens," and **Tim is youngest yet "late teens"** and turns 18 in Book 3
  — which can't all be true at once (a youngest who is also late-teens while an
  older sister is 13–14).

**Recommendation:** fix canonical birth years for the three half-siblings once,
in `characters.md`, with ages-per-book, and reconcile ch07/ch08/ch14/ch15/ch19
to it. Anchor on the only hard constraint: **Tim is the youngest and turns 18 in
Book 3.** Everything else should be derived from that.

### 2.4 Jay's employment history **[MED]**

- ch01 (Leo): "worked **three years at a forgettable mid-tier firm**." Jay's own
  interiority that chapter references only "my last job, at a mid-tier consulting
  firm."
- ch02 S5 (Ian reading the file): "university education, **six years at three
  firms**."
- ch06 S3 (PI report): harassment at **his first company** (HR ignored it) *and*
  a separate quit from **his last job** over a coworker's advances — i.e. at
  least two prior employers with abuse.

The PI report and the craft-notes intent ("he believes leaving his **first and
previous** jobs was cowardice") imply the **six-years/three-firms** version is
canon. Ch01's "three years at a … firm" is the outlier and should be widened, or
at least not stated so narrowly, so Leo's throwaway line doesn't contradict the
file Ian reads one chapter later.

### 2.5 Ian's smoking **[LOW]**

ch05 S3 has Jay notice "a faint smell of cigarettes" on Ian during the Hartwell
crunch — i.e. Ian smokes under stress. ch17 S2 then frames lighting one as
breaking a promise: "He'd **quit smoking years ago**." Both are stress-triggered,
so it's *roughly* consistent, but "quit years ago" slightly oversells it given
we already smelled smoke on him in November. Soften one or the other (e.g. ch17
"he'd all but quit").

### 2.6 Walk-on registry gap: "Jen" **[LOW]**

`characters.md`'s named-walk-on registry is meant to stop name reuse, and it
catches Sal (ch05). But **Jen from accounting** — who appears in **ch05 S1**
("Jen from accounting was making eyes at him") *and recurs* in **ch17** flirting
with Jay at the sunset dinner — is **not** in the registry. She's a recurring
named minor and should be logged. ("Allen," the ex-colleague in Luc/Kim's ch07
anecdote, is also named and unlogged — lower priority since he never appears.)

### 2.7 What's solidly consistent (keep)

Worth recording so we don't "fix" things that are fine:

- The **Sam Connelly math** holds: betrayed at 25, thirteen years ago, "best
  eight months," secretary/spy → that's exactly *why* an **assistant** terrifies
  him; Ian is 38, thirteen years at Turner. All internally consistent.
- **Luc as a former "joke hire" who became competent** is set up in ch01 (Leo's
  "Luc turned out surprisingly competent") and paid off in ch03/ch04/ch06.
- **Cruver → "Ric Cruver"** (ch13) matches the registry; **Zoe's dive
  certification** (ch17) matches her profile; **Amy's daughters 15 & 13** is
  consistent ch-to-ch.

## 3. Event plausibility ("fictional but plausible")

The world is an elite boutique finance firm, so opulence is the baseline. The
spine of Book 1 — wounded people growing close through daily work — is plausible
and well-built. The plausibility strain is concentrated in a handful of
**wish-fulfillment levers** and one **thriller-grade** plot.

### 3.1 The Orrey / Cruver takedown **[MED — biggest outlier]**

In ch10→ch13, Cruver gropes Jay once at the gala; in response Ian spends three
months and, working essentially alone (public filings, "anonymous tips to
regulatory agencies," cooperating ex-employees, "sources who kept him
informed"), **surfaces securities fraud, money-laundering and foreign-bribery**
at Orrey Capital, triggers a **federal investigation**, gets the client
terminated, and by Friday it's **national news** with the firm collapsing.

Problems:

- **Disproportion.** A single grope escalates to a company-ending federal case.
- **Speed & solo capability.** Real securities investigations don't spin up to
  "federal, public, national news" in three months off one executive's
  after-hours sleuthing.
- **Convenience.** Orrey *happens* to be deeply, provably criminal. ch13 even
  lampshades it ("most companies have irregularities if you know where to look")
  and then escalates straight to bribery anyway.
- **Tone.** It briefly makes Ian *omnipotent*, which cuts against the book's own
  texture (per craft-notes, Turner is a sanctuary and the real threats are
  internalized). Ian's other protective acts are human-scale (the car ride, the
  watch); this one is a thriller dropped into a slow-burn.

Not necessarily *wrong* — it lands emotionally and gives Jay catharsis — but
it's the one event a careful reader will call implausible. If you want to keep
it, consider dialing the *mechanism* down (Ian **surfaces** existing
irregularities and **feeds** an inquiry that was already stirring, rather than
manufacturing a federal case), which preserves the satisfaction without making
him a one-man enforcement agency.

### 3.2 The retreat room-share device **[MED]**

ch14: HR institutes "mentor rooming" so **everyone shares**, and Leo engineers
the **CFO sharing a room with his assistant**. Craft-notes already flag this as
"the most device-y beat in an otherwise organic book"; from a plausibility angle
it's also the least believable *logistically* — a firm that books private rooms
as the norm suddenly making the C-suite bunk with subordinates is an HR/liability
oddity, and "all the C-suite are participating" is pure hand-wave. It works
because we *want* it to. Worth at least a sturdier in-world reason, or leaning
into Leo's machination openly enough that no one's pretending it's real policy.

### 3.3 The company "limousine" lent for a family trip **[MED]**

ch18–19: an executive **assistant** borrows a chauffeur-less luxury sedan/"limo"
"worth more than his apartment" under "Section twelve, fleet vehicle policies"
to drive four hours home for a middle-school graduation — then ferries his
sister's friends around town in it. Companies don't generally lend six-figure
vehicles to assistants for personal joyrides. It's a charm beat (and the
sister's awe is the point), but it's a stretch. A pool car that's merely *nice*
would do the same comedic work with less strain.

### 3.4 The compensation fantasy **[MED — keep an eye on it]**

Jay, an EA, receives a monthly base ~2–3× his old salary **plus** a standard
monthly bonus, **plus** quarterly bonuses, **plus** an **$80k** year-end bonus
(ch09), itemized in ch19 as "CFO Desk Premium," "Monthly Operations Supplement,"
"Discretionary Quarterly Float." This is the book's core wish-fulfillment lever
and it's *load-bearing* (the "joke hire" comedy, the wardrobe arc, Leo's
retention philosophy). It's fine as a deliberate fantasy — but note the internal
friction: ch20's Max line "**you're doing analyst work for assistant pay**"
sits oddly against a package that already pays Jay like a senior analyst. Either
trim the "assistant pay" framing or keep the comp lavish but stop calling it
underpayment.

### 3.5 Smaller plausibility / craft snags **[LOW]**

- **Two back-to-back winter storms** as the same obstacle: ch11 S1 (snowstorm
  wrecks the commute) then ch12 S1 (ice storm wrecks the commute → the Hathaway's
  emergency purchase). Reads slightly recycled; consider differentiating them or
  merging the "weather defeats Jay's commute" function.
- **The founding premise is a little convenient:** Jay finds the firm via a
  "gossipy online thread" about Ian being openly gay and famously immune to
  office romance, and applies "on a wing and a prayer." Acceptable as setup —
  but see the underused thread below.
- **Gala obliviousness:** a circle of guests stands around chatting while Cruver
  openly strokes Jay's spine/waist and whispers in his ear (ch10). Crowd-noise
  covers a lot, but the group's total non-reaction is convenient.

## 4. Underused setup (not an error, but a missed lever) **[LOW]**

Jay deliberately chose this firm **because** Ian is known to be gay and immune to
advances — that's his safety filter in ch01. Yet across the year, as Jay starts
noticing Ian's cologne, his physique, the charged late evenings, his POV **never
once routes through that knowledge** ("my boss is gay; what does it mean that I
keep reacting to him?"). It can read as consistent with his denial — but it's a
loaded gun introduced on page one that never goes off in his interiority. One
half-beat of Jay flinching away from that exact thought would both honor the
setup and deepen the denial.
