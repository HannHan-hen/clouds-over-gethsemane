# Chapter 10: The high finance

## Scene 1: The Grand Gala

The night of the gala arrived with unseasonable warmth for January. Jay spent an hour preparing, adjusting his bow tie multiple times and checking his appearance from every angle. The salon had worked wonders with his hair, creating a polished style that enhanced his features without looking overdone.

Still, staring at his reflection, Jay felt ridiculous. The formal wear was perfect, his grooming impeccable, but he still couldn't shake the sensation of playing dress-up.

The taxi ride to the Grand Hotel gave him time to compose himself. Through the window, he watched the city—people heading to dinner, couples walking hand in hand. It felt comforting after the surreal experience of getting ready for such an elegant event.

The Grand Hotel's entrance was ablaze with lights, red carpet extending from the street to the lobby doors. Photographers flanked the walkway, capturing arrivals for society pages and business publications. Jay paid the driver and stepped onto the pavement, immediately feeling intimidated by the spectacle.

Inside, the ballroom was breathtaking. Crystal chandeliers cast warm light over hundreds of guests in formal attire. Women wore stunning gowns in rich colors. Men in perfectly tailored tuxedos stood in groups, discussing business.

Jay searched frantically for familiar faces. The sheer scale of the event was staggering—at least three times larger than the quarterly gathering he'd attended months earlier. This wasn't just a company party, it was a showcase of the industry's elite.

"Jay!" Luc's voice cut through the ambient noise. "Over here!"

Jay spotted his colleague near the bar, looking comfortable and relaxed in his formal wear. He made his way through the crowd, relieved to find an anchor point.

"You look fantastic," Luc said, adjusting his own bow tie. "The tuxedo suits you perfectly."

"Thank you," Jay said, tugging at his cuffs. "I feel like a penguin."

"A very handsome penguin," Luc said with a grin.

"I feel completely out of place."

"Everyone does at these things. The trick is pretending otherwise until you believe it."

Jay tried to follow this advice, but the grandeur of the event made it difficult. Waiters circulated with champagne, while the conversations around him involved mergers, acquisitions, and nine-figure deals discussed as casually as weather reports.

Across the room, Ian stood with a group of distinguished-looking men, his posture perfect and expression attentive. Even from a distance, he commanded attention, radiating confidence that came from years of success in this world.

Their eyes met briefly, and Jay felt a flutter in his stomach. Ian was momentarily speechless. The young man looked stunning, the tuxedo's lines emphasizing his slender frame. There was something endearing about Jay's bearing, slightly uncertain but determined to rise to the occasion. The tailor had done exceptional work, and whoever had styled Jay's hair deserved recognition. Even Jay's obvious nervousness only added to his appeal, creating an almost irresistible combination of sophistication and vulnerability.

"Earth to Ian," Leo's voice cut through his thoughts. "You're staring."

Ian turned to find his friend watching him with undisguised amusement.

"I wasn't staring," Ian said coolly. "I was observing the guests."

"Right. One guest in particular." Leo followed Ian's previous direction and spotted Jay. "Oh, he does look good. Almost too good for this crowd of sharks. Half the people here are already noticing him. He's attractive and clearly out of his element. Someone's going to make a move."

Ian felt something twist in his stomach. "That's not my concern."

"Isn't it?" Leo's smile widened. "You should probably go protect your poor assistant before someone sweeps him off his feet."

"Don't be ridiculous."

"I'm being practical. He's your responsibility tonight, isn't he? Part of your team?"

Ian glanced back toward Jay. The young man was handling himself well, but Ian could see the tension in his shoulders.

"He can take care of himself."

"Of course he can. But wouldn't it be terrible if some smooth-talking investment banker decided to show him around? You know how persuasive those types can be."

Ian's jaw tightened. "You're being absurd."

"Am I? Because I see at least three potential suitors eyeing him right now." Leo gestured discretely toward different groups. "The tall one by the bar has been watching him since he arrived. The silver-haired gentleman near the windows keeps glancing over. And don't get me started on that young partner from Greymount—he's practically circling like a predator."

Ian followed Leo's indications and felt his irritation spike. His friend wasn't wrong—Jay had attracted attention, and not all of it felt benign.

"Stop talking."

Leo's delighted laugh made several nearby guests turn their heads. "You're jealous. This is wonderful."

"I'm not jealous. I'm concerned about appropriate behaviour."

"Sure you are. Which is why you should go rescue the poor puppy before someone else does."

Ian shot Leo a withering look. "Don't call him that."

"Why not? He does have that adorable lost look. Very appealing to the protective instincts."

"You're insufferable."

"I'm helpful. Go claim your territory before it's too late."

Ian opened his mouth to deliver a cutting response, then stopped. Across the room, Jay was indeed being approached by the silver-haired man Leo had mentioned—a partner from a competing firm known for his aggressive recruitment tactics.

"Fine," Ian said abruptly. "I'll go ensure he's comfortable."

"Of course you will," Leo said, his expression triumphant. "How noble of you."

Ian straightened his shoulders and moved across the room, telling himself he was simply being a responsible supervisor. The fact that his pulse had quickened at the sight of Jay in formal wear was entirely irrelevant.

"Excuse me," Ian said, appearing beside Jay with grace. "I hope you're both enjoying the evening."

"Ian," Jay said, relief evident in his voice. "Yes, it's magnificent."

"Magnificent indeed," the man agreed.

"If you'll excuse us, I need to introduce my colleagues to some of our other clients."

Without waiting for a response, Ian guided Jay and Luc toward the buffet area where Max and Amy were already stationed, plates filled with elegant appetizers.

"Thank goodness," Jay murmured as they walked. "I had no idea what to say to him."

"You were doing fine," Ian said, though privately he appreciated the excuse to intervene. "Some guests can be... intense."

"That's one word for it," Luc added.

They reached the buffet where Max greeted them cheerfully. "There you are! I was beginning to think you'd abandoned us for more glamorous company."

"Never," Ian said, surprising himself with how much he meant it. "How are you finding the evening?"

"Excellent food, decent wine, and enough networking opportunities to last a lifetime," Amy replied. "Though I have to say, Jay, you look great."

Jay flushed at the compliment. "Thank you. I feel like I'm playing dress-up."

"We all are," Max said. "That's half the fun."

Ian remained with the group instead of mingling with the industry leaders as he typically would. Usually, these events were strategic opportunities for building relationships and identifying new business prospects. Tonight, however, he felt more interested in ensuring his team's comfort than advancing his own agenda.

"This is unprecedented," Leo said, appearing with a fresh drink and an expression of delighted surprise. "Ian Hagen, master networker, holding court with his own staff instead of wooing potential clients."

"The evening is young," Ian replied, though he made no move to leave the group.

"Indeed it is," Leo agreed, his smile widening. "Indeed it is."

Jay stood slightly behind Ian, unconsciously using him as a shield against the rest of the room. Ian's broad shoulders and confident bearing created a pocket of calm in the chaos, making the evening feel more manageable.

## Scene 2: The incident

Ian was inevitably drawn into conversations with key clients and the strategic networking that made these events worthwhile. Jay watched him move through the crowd, commanding attention without seeming to seek it.

"Come on," Max said, appearing at Jay's elbow. "Let me introduce you to some people."

Before Jay could protest, he got swept into a group of distinguished guests. Max made introductions with enthusiasm, presenting Jay as "one of our most promising analysts" to a rotating cast of clients and business partners.

"Jay, meet Meg Randon from Pacific Investments," Max said, guiding him toward an elegant woman in a burgundy gown. "Meg, Jay has been instrumental in our recent acquisition."

The conversations moved from one introduction to the next. Jay tried to keep up, giving rehearsed responses about company performance. The guests seemed interested in his insights, which was both flattering and terrifying.

"Here, you need a drink," said a man, pressing a champagne flute into Jay's hand. "Can't discuss business without proper lubrication."

Jay accepted the glass, taking small sips as the conversations continued. Another guest immediately brought another one when he finished, then another handed him a different cocktail. The alcohol helped ease his nerves, making the intimidating crowd seem more manageable.

"You're much younger than I expected," commented a woman with pearls and a charming smile. "Ian certainly knows how to pick them."

Jay wasn't sure how to respond to that, so he simply smiled and took another sip of his drink. The room felt warmer now, the conversations louder and more animated, everything slightly out of focus.

"Such a refreshing change," another client whose name Jay had already forgotten, commented, watching Jay with uncomfortable intensity. "Most young people in this business are so aggressive, so hungry. You have a gentler disposition."

Jay managed a polite smile, though the man's tone made him uneasy.

"Absolutely charming," the same client continued, moving closer. "You know, you remind me of someone I used to know. Same delicate features, same innocent expression."

The man's hand landed on Jay's shoulder. Jay stepped back, but the circle of people around him made retreat impossible.

"Such pretty eyes," the client continued, his voice dropping to an intimate murmur. "I bet you get a lot of attention with looks like that."

Jay's chest tightened. The man's hand moved to his back, fingers trailing along his spine, making his skin crawl. Other members of the group continued talking, apparently oblivious to the inappropriate contact.

"You're very young, aren't you?" the client asked, his breath warm against Jay's ear. "Fresh out of university, I'd guess. Still learning the ropes."

Jay tried to respond, but his voice caught. The man's hand had moved to his waist, thumb stroking the fabric of his jacket. The touch felt predatory, reducing Jay to nothing more than an object for this powerful man's entertainment.

"I should—" Jay began, but the client interrupted.

"No need to be shy, beautiful. In this business, connections matter. A young man like you could benefit from the right kind of guidance."

The implications hung heavy in the air. Jay felt his composure cracking under the weight of humiliation and powerlessness. He was trapped again, prey for someone who saw his youth and perceived weakness as invitation. The alcohol that had earlier provided confidence now made him feel dizzy.

"I should probably find my colleagues."

"Oh, don't rush off. We're just getting acquainted. You know, I have several opportunities coming up that might suit someone with your... particular talents. Perhaps we could discuss them privately sometime."

"I appreciate the offer, but I'm very happy in my current position."

"Are you? How refreshing. Such loyalty is rare in someone so young and attractive." The man's other hand touched Jay's arm, fingers trailing along the fabric. "Though I wonder if Ian fully appreciates what he has. Such a waste if he doesn't."

Jay could feel tears welling up. The man's hands, his suggestive tone, the way he looked at Jay like he was something to be consumed—it all felt horribly familiar.

"Excuse me," Jay whispered, pulling away abruptly. "I need to use the restroom."

He fled, weaving through the crowd toward the hotel's facilities. His hands shook as he pushed through the bathroom door, relieved to find it empty.

Jay stared at his reflection in the mirror, trying to compose himself. He was flushed, his breathing unsteady. He splashed cold water on his face, hoping to calm his racing pulse.

Shame washed over him—the same helpless feeling he'd experienced at his first job. All his progress, all his growing confidence, seemed to evaporate in the face of this casual violation.

Jay locked himself in a stall, pressing his back against the door while trying to control his breathing. "You're being ridiculous," he told himself. "Nothing actually happened." But the attempts at rationalization did nothing. It still felt like a violation, even if nothing explicitly inappropriate had occurred.

Jay stayed in the stall for several minutes, composing himself enough to face the world again. When he finally emerged, he nearly collided with Ian in the hallway.

"There you are. I've been looking for you."

Jay opened his mouth to speak, but Ian's expression stopped him. He looked furious, his jaw clenched and eyes blazing with barely controlled anger.

"I'm sorry," Jay said quickly, shrinking back. "I didn't mean to disappear. I just needed a moment."

Ian's expression immediately softened. "You have nothing to apologize for. Are you alright?"

"Yes, I'm fine. Just needed some air."

Ian looked at Jay's face—the red-rimmed eyes, the trembling hands. "No, you're not fine. What happened?"

"Nothing happened. Really, I'm just tired."

"Jay. You don't have to tell me anything, but please don't pretend you're fine when you're obviously not."

"I'm sorry. I'm probably overreacting. It's just that these events can be overwhelming."

"They can be uncomfortable, yes. But they're not supposed to be traumatizing." Ian's tone held an edge of steel. "Did someone upset you?"

"It was probably nothing. Just a misunderstanding."

Ian's expression darkened again, but he kept his voice level. "Would you like to leave? I can have my driver take you home."

"That's not necessary. I can get a taxi."

"I insist. And I'll come with you, if you don't mind. Someone here has thoroughly irritated me tonight, and I need a reason to leave before I do something unprofessional."

Jay looked surprised. "You want to leave too?"

"Desperately. I was one inappropriate comment away from punching someone in the face in front of half the financial district." Ian's slight smile took the edge off his words. "You'd actually be doing me a favor."

The admission made Jay feel less pathetic. "I find that hard to believe."

"You'd be surprised. I have a very short fuse when it comes to certain types of behaviour. Come on, let's go"

"If you're sure it's not too much trouble."

"None at all. Let me call my driver."

Without drawing attention, Ian guided Jay through a staff door at the rear of the hotel, where a black car stood ready for them. Their discreet departure blended in with the flow of hotel staff coming and going.

## Scene 3: Soothing the fears

The car's interior was luxurious but not ostentatious. Jay sank into the cushions, finally allowing himself to relax.

"You know," Ian said as they pulled into traffic, "I once attended a similar event where a client spent the entire evening trying to convince me to invest in his nephew's cryptocurrency venture. Apparently, it was going to revolutionize the pet grooming industry."

Jay looked at him in surprise. "Really?"

"Completely serious. He cornered me by the seafood station for forty minutes, explaining how blockchain technology would transform dog bathing. I finally escaped by claiming I was allergic to shellfish and needed immediate medical attention."

Despite everything, Jay smiled. "Did it work?"

"Beautifully. Though I had to avoid the seafood station for the rest of the evening, which was unfortunate because they had excellent crab cakes."

Ian continued with similar stories—absurd client requests, social faux pas at formal events, the occasional spectacular failure of decorum. His dry humour and timing gradually dissolved the tension in Jay's shoulders.

"Here we are," Ian said as the car stopped in front of Jay's building.

Jay looked at the modest apartment complex, suddenly acutely aware of how different it was from Ian's world. "Thank you for the ride. And for... everything."

"It was my pleasure. I hope you feel better tomorrow."

Jay hesitated at the car door. "Would you like to come up for tea? I know it's late, but..."

Ian paused, clearly weighing the invitation. "If you're sure it wouldn't be too much trouble."

"No trouble at all."

Jay led the way through the building's lobby to the stairwell, acutely conscious of the contrast between this and the elegant hotel they'd just left. "I'm sorry there's no elevator. It's four flights up."

"I don't mind stairs," Ian said, following without complaint.

Jay watched him climb with ease despite his formal attire. He was clearly in excellent physical condition, making the four flights seem effortless.

"This is it," Jay said, unlocking his apartment door. "It's not much, but it's home."

Ian entered the small but tidy space, taking in the simple furniture and careful organization. "It's very comfortable. You've made it welcoming."

Jay busied himself in the kitchen, glad to have something to do with his hands. "I hope you don't mind basic tea. I'm not much of a host."

"Basic tea sounds perfect."

Ian sat on the small couch, somehow making the apartment feel more sophisticated instead of highlighting its limitations. When Jay brought the tea, Ian accepted it with the same grace he'd shown at the elegant gala.

"How long have you lived here?" Ian asked, making conversation.

"About six years. I found it right after I moved to the city." Jay curled up in the armchair across from him. "It's not glamorous, but the rent is reasonable and the neighbourhood is safe."

"Comfort matters more than luxury in most cases. Was having a place to yourself important to you?"

"Yes. I know it's more expensive than having roommates, but I value the privacy. My previous financial situation didn't really allow for anything better than this studio."

"Privacy is worth the premium. Especially after long days at work."

"Exactly. I could probably afford something closer to the office now, maybe even a proper one-bedroom, but I'm used to this area. There are nice parks nearby, good cafes. It feels like home."

"That's more valuable than square footage. A lengthy commute can be draining, but if you're comfortable here..."

"I actually enjoy the metro ride. Gives me time to think, or read. Though I suppose you don't use public transport much."

"More than you might think. Sometimes it's faster than dealing with traffic. Speaking of adjustments, how did you find tonight's event? I know these corporate functions can be intimidating."

"It was... intense. My previous jobs never had anything like that, so I'm still learning how to handle them. The networking aspect especially."

"You handled yourself very well. Better than most people at their first major gala."

"Really?" Jay looked surprised. "I felt completely out of my depth. All those important people, discussing deals worth millions..."

"That's perfectly normal. The key is listening more than speaking, which you did naturally. Most people try too hard to impress and end up saying something they regret."

"I was terrified of saying the wrong thing to the wrong person."

"A healthy fear. But you were polite and interested in what others had to say. That's rarer than you might think in our industry."

Jay felt a warmth spread through his chest at the praise. "Thank you. That means a lot coming from you."

"How did you find the scale of it? I imagine it was quite different from smaller company events."

"Completely different. The quarterly meeting felt manageable, but tonight..." Jay shook his head. "The ballroom was enormous, and everyone seemed to know exactly who they should be talking to and why."

"That comes with experience. And frankly, half of those people are just as uncertain as you were—they're simply better at hiding it."

Jay laughed softly. "That's oddly comforting."

"The real secret is that most networking is just extended small talk with business cards. You're naturally good at putting people at ease, which is more valuable than knowing every industry insider."

"I hadn't thought of it that way."

Ian drank his tea, letting the quiet stretch out before setting the cup down and looking at Jay. "Are you feeling better about the evening? I know it ended on a difficult note."

Jay's expression sobered slightly. "Much better, thank you. I was worried I'd embarrassed myself, or worse, reflected poorly on the company."

"Not at all. You represented Turner & Associates admirably. And if anyone made you uncomfortable, that's entirely their failing, not yours."

"I appreciate you saying that."

"Of course." Ian said, then added noticing Jay's fatigue. "I should probably head home. Thank you for the tea, and for giving me an excuse to leave early."

"Thank you for making sure I got home safely. And for the stories. They helped more than you know."

"Jay, if anyone makes you uncomfortable at future events, please don't hesitate to find me immediately. These functions can be challenging, but you shouldn't have to endure harassment."

Jay nodded, touched by the concern. "I will. Thank you."

After Ian left, Jay sat in his apartment, processing the evening's events. The incident still bothered him, but Ian's kindness had turned what could have been a traumatic memory into something more manageable. He'd seen a different side of Ian tonight—warmer, even playful in his storytelling. Jay felt like he might have found not just a mentor, but someone who cared about his well-being.

Meanwhile, Ian sat in his car outside Jay's building, wrestling with his own thoughts. He'd overstepped tonight, but seeing Jay distressed had overridden every other consideration. The fury he'd felt still simmered beneath his composed exterior.

He'd handled it appropriately, he told himself. He'd helped a colleague through a difficult situation. Nothing more. The fact that Jay looked beautiful in his formal wear, that his vulnerability had stirred protectiveness in Ian's chest, that sitting in his modest apartment had felt more comfortable than any elegant venue—none of that mattered. Ian finally instructed his driver to take him home, but the image of Jay's smile lingered long after the evening ended.

## Scene 4: Aftermath

Jay arrived at the office early Monday morning, as had become his habit. It felt comforting after the weekend's emotional upheaval. He'd spent Sunday processing the gala's events, alternating between embarrassment about his reaction and gratitude for Ian's kindness.

Ian walked in at five minutes to eight.

"Good morning, Ian. Your nine o'clock meeting has been moved to nine-thirty, and the reports are ready for your review."

"Thank you, Jay. I trust you had a pleasant weekend?"

"Yes, very restful. I hope yours was as well."

"It was. Thank you for asking."

The exchange was perfectly normal and unremarkable. Jay felt both relief and an odd sense of disappointment. He'd wondered if Saturday's events might change their dynamic somehow, but Ian's composed demeanour made it clear that nothing was different. He had simply been kind to a distressed colleague—nothing more, nothing less.

Jay was thankful for Ian's discretion. The last thing he wanted was awkwardness or pity affecting their working relationship. Still, part of him had hoped for some acknowledgement of the connection he'd felt during their conversation in his apartment.

As the morning progressed, the office filled with people. But instead of diving into work, everyone seemed focused on dissecting the gala's events. Jay heard fragments of conversations from every direction—praise for the venue, critiques of the food, speculation about various business deals discussed.

"Did you see Pat's dress?" Ela was saying to Amy as Jay passed their desks. "That shade of emerald was absolutely stunning on her."

"The food was incredible," Max added, joining their conversation. "Though I think I ate enough appetizers to feed a small country."

"The real mystery is why Ian left so early. He always stays until the bitter end at these things," Ela said, perching on the edge of Zoe's desk.

"Maybe he had another engagement," Zoe replied. The comment held no real conviction. Everyone knew how unlikely that was, but she wasn't one to speculate.

"Or maybe he was just tired of schmoozing. Even Ian has his limits," Max added.

Jay concentrated on his screen, hoping his face wouldn't betray anything. Clearly, Ian had been discreet about their departure—no one seemed to have noticed Jay's early exit or connected it to Ian's.

Kim appeared at Jay's desk with a grin. "So, how did you survive your first major gala? You looked terrified when I saw you earlier in the evening."

"I drank too much," Jay said, sticking to the simple explanation he'd prepared. "Spent half the evening in the bathroom."

The admission prompted a round of laughter from nearby colleagues.

"Welcome to the club. We've all been there. Remember Max's last gala?" Luc said.

"Hey now. That champagne was stronger than advertised," Max protested, but he was smiling.

"You had to be practically carried out. Leo found you asleep in the coat check," Amy reminded him.

"At least I didn't spill red wine on the mayor's wife. That was Kim's contribution to last year's festivities," Ela added.

"It was an accident! Though I still think she deserved it after that comment about tech workers," Kim responded.

Jay listened to the stories with growing relief. His own embarrassment seemed minor compared to the spectacular mishaps being shared. Everyone had mortifying moments at these events—it was apparently part of the experience.

"At least these galas only happen once a year," Luc said, appearing at Jay's desk with a fresh cup of coffee. "Though I have to admit, the networking opportunities are valuable."

"True," Jay agreed. "I met several interesting people before... well, before I overindulged."

"The trick is pacing yourself. Start with wine, switch to water between drinks, avoid the stronger cocktails entirely. Though I suppose learning these lessons the hard way is part of the experience."

Jay nodded, accepting the advice while privately knowing alcohol hadn't been the real problem. The memory of the incident still made his stomach twist, but the normalcy of the office setting helped push those thoughts into the background.

"The important thing is that we all survived to tell the tale. And the gossip from these things keeps us entertained for months," Ela said sagely.

"Speaking of gossip," Kim said, lowering his voice conspiratorially, "did anyone else notice that Cruver spent the entire evening trying to corner people by the bar? He was like a predator stalking prey."

Jay's hands stilled on his keyboard. Cruver. He hadn't been able to recall the name before, but now that it was mentioned, he was almost certain that was the man.

"Cruver's always been handsy. Someone should report him eventually," Amy said with distaste.

"To whom? His own firm? They'd just cover it up," Luc asked.

"Maybe, but his behavior is getting worse. Someone's going to call him out publicly sooner or later."

The conversation drifted to other topics, but Jay thought about Cruver's aggressive behaviour. Had other people noticed? Had he been equally inappropriate with others?

Throughout the morning, Ian acted as if nothing had happened. The only difference Jay noticed was an occasional glance in his direction—subtle looks that lasted perhaps a second longer than normal.

Jay told himself he was imagining things. Ian was simply ensuring his assistant was managing his workload properly. Nothing more complicated than that.

By lunch, the gala's aftermath had already turned into office legend. Stories would be retold and embellished over the coming weeks, but the immediate excitement was fading. Jay was glad that his own experience had been absorbed into the general chaos without attracting attention.

By the end of the day, Jay thought about how normal everything felt. The weekend's emotional turmoil might have been a dream, except for the lingering warmth he felt when remembering Ian's kindness. Ian had seen him at his most vulnerable and responded with compassion.

Jay was content to let the incident fade into memory, another workplace challenge resolved. He had no way of knowing that Ian's thoughts were far from calm.

In his office, Ian made several discrete phone calls throughout the day. By evening, he'd confirmed Cruver's identity and begun researching his business relationships. Turner & Associates did business with his company, but that didn't make him untouchable.

He would do everything in his power to ensure Cruver paid for his behaviour. He had made a mistake targeting Jay, and Ian intended to make sure he regretted it.

The plan would take time to implement properly. Ian couldn't act impulsively without risking his own standing. But Cruver would learn that some actions had severe consequences, even if they initially seemed trivial.

As Jay prepared to leave for the day, Ian watched him organize his desk in the same way he always did. He seemed to have recovered from Saturday's distress, but Ian couldn't forget the terror in his eyes. The image had lodged deeply, surfacing whenever he tried to focus on work.

He knew he was overreacting. The situation was under control, Jay was safe now. His own response troubled him—the anger, the protectiveness. It wasn't simple concern anymore. The thought of anyone taking advantage of Jay left his pulse spiking.

Jay deserved to work in an environment free from predatory behaviour. Ian would make sure that happened.

## Scene 5: Choice

January turned into February with the relentless pace of new year business. The office was overwhelmed with the endless stream of reports that marked the beginning of another fiscal cycle. Jay was busy managing Ian's increasingly complex schedule while handling the administrative tasks that kept the department running smoothly.

HR flooded everyone's inboxes with reminders about upcoming deadlines. Performance reviews needed to be scheduled, annual leave requests submitted, and various compliance training modules completed. The gala felt like a distant memory, which suited Jay perfectly. He'd successfully avoided thinking about Cruver's inappropriate behavior, though Ian's protective response that evening remained a warm memory he revisited more often than he cared to admit.

"Jay," Ian said on one morning at the beginning of February, "could you schedule a performance review for us? It's been six months since you started."

Jay looked up from his computer, surprised by how quickly the time had passed. "Of course. When would be convenient?"

"Tomorrow afternoon, if possible. Two o'clock."

Jay added the appointment to Ian's calendar, feeling nervous. Performance reviews at his previous job had been perfunctory affairs, brief meetings where his supervisor gave generic feedback before moving on. He suspected Ian's approach would be more comprehensive.

The next afternoon, Jay knocked on Ian's office door at two o'clock, holding a notepad and wearing his most attentive expression.

"Please, sit," Ian said, gesturing to the chair across from his desk. "I want to discuss your progress over the past six months."

Jay sat on the chair, trying to read Ian's mood. He looked serious.

"First, I want to acknowledge that you've exceeded expectations in every area. Your organizational skills and ability to anticipate needs have been exemplary. You've made my job much easier."

Jay felt warmth spread through his chest. "Thank you. I've tried to be scrupulous."

"You've been more than that. You've been intuitive. That's a rare quality in this industry. Your client interaction skills have also improved dramatically."

"I appreciate the feedback."

"Which brings me to my main point. I believe you're ready for advancement. I'd like to offer you a position as a regular team member. It would mean a salary increase and expanded responsibilities."

Jay was stunned. "A promotion?"

"Yes. You'd be working directly on analysis projects and strategic planning. It's a natural progression for someone with your capabilities."

Jay's mind raced. A promotion meant recognition, advancement, everything he'd dreamed of when he first started. But it also meant leaving his current role, no longer working directly for Ian.

"I... I don't know what to say."

"You don't need to decide immediately. But I wanted you to know that the opportunity exists. Luc followed the same path. He started as my assistant and moved up to regular team member within six months."

"But Luc had much better credentials than I do."

"Credentials matter less than performance. And your performance has been exceptional. You seem hesitant. May I ask why?"

Jay struggled to find words that wouldn't reveal too much. "I'm not sure I'm ready for that level of responsibility. I enjoy my current work, and I'm still learning so much in this role."

His real reasons were more complicated. Jay wanted to continue working directly for Ian, wanted to remain the person Ian relied on for daily support. The thought of being reassigned to work with other team members, of losing the close collaboration they'd developed, filled him with unexpected dread.

Ian watched Jay's internal struggle. Privately, he was overjoyed by Jay's hesitation. Working with Jay had become one of the most enjoyable aspects of his life. The younger man's dedication made every day more pleasant.

But Ian's integrity overrode his personal preferences. "I understand your caution, but I'd encourage you to consider the opportunity. Career advancement is important, especially at your age."

Jay's discomfort was evident in his posture. "Could I think about it?"

"Of course. Take all the time you need. And Jay? This is just a proposal. If you decide you prefer your current role, I'll be more than happy to retain your services. You're invaluable in this position."

Jay felt relief wash over him. "Thank you. I really do appreciate the offer."

"Consider it carefully. But know that either choice will be respected."

When Jay returned to his desk, he found it difficult to concentrate. The promotion was objectively the right choice—better pay, clearer advancement opportunities. But something in his chest rebelled against the idea of leaving Ian's direct service.

"Hey," Luc said, appearing at his desk. "How did the review go?"

"Good. He offered me a promotion to regular team member."

Luc's eyebrows shot up. "That's fantastic! Congratulations."

"I haven't accepted yet."

"What? Why not?"

Jay fumbled for an explanation. "I'm not sure I'm ready. I like my current work."

"Jay, that's career advancement. You can't stay an assistant forever."

"I know, I just... I want to think about it."

By the end of the week, word had spread through the office. Max congratulated him over lunch, Amy shared advice about transitioning to analytical work.

"So when do you start in your new role?" Zoe asked during a coffee break.

"I haven't decided yet," Jay admitted.

The team exchanged glances. It was clear they found his hesitation puzzling, but no one pressed for details.

That evening, Jay sat in his apartment, weighing his options. The logical choice was obvious—accept the promotion, advance his career, prove himself capable of growth. But his heart pulled him in a different direction.

He thought about Ian, about the joy of being the person Ian turned to when everything else went wrong. The role gave him a sense of purpose beyond what career advancement could offer.

More than that, he'd observed Ian's isolation within the office. He shouldered tremendous responsibility, made difficult decisions, and bore the weight of the department's success with minimal support. Jay wanted to be that support, to continue making Ian's life easier. He didn't want to advance away from Ian, he wanted to advance alongside him. And if that meant remaining an assistant, so be it.

Monday morning, Jay knocked on Ian's office door. "I've made my decision about the promotion."

Ian looked up from his computer. "And?"

"I'd like to stay in my current role, if that's acceptable."

"May I ask why?"

Jay had prepared for this question. "I enjoy the work. I feel like I'm still learning, and I want to master this role completely before moving on to something else."

It wasn't the complete truth, but it wasn't a lie either. Ian watched him in silence for a long moment.

"I respect that decision. But I want you to know that the opportunity will remain open. If you change your mind in the future, we can revisit the conversation."

"Thank you. I appreciate that."

As Jay returned to his desk, he felt a sense of rightness about his choice. It might not be the most ambitious path, but it felt authentic to who he was and what he wanted.

Ian watched Jay leave, fighting to suppress his relief. Having Jay continue as his assistant was a selfish preference, but one that made his workdays immeasurably better.

For Jay, the decision marked another milestone in his journey at Turner & Associates. His skills had been acknowledged, he'd been offered advancement, and he'd made a choice based on his own values. It felt like growth, even if it meant staying in the same role.
