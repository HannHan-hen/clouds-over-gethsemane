# Chapter 11: February rush

## Scene 1: Snow and scent

February began with a big drop in temperature and a massive snowfall that blanketed the city. Jay watched from his apartment window as the flakes continued to fall, thick and relentless, coating everything in pristine white. The beauty was undeniable, but he knew the commute would be brutal.

His prediction was correct. The metro crawled through the tunnels at half speed, announcements crackling over the intercom about weather-related delays. Jay fidgeted impatiently in his seat, checking his phone repeatedly. He normally arrived at the office by seven-thirty, giving himself a comfortable buffer before the workday began. Today he wouldn't make it until ten minutes before eight.

Still early by most standards, but later than he'd like. The deviation made him uncomfortable.

The Northlight Financial Center emerged from the snow like a gleaming beacon glowing against the gray morning sky. Jay hurried through the lobby, his old coat doing little to shield him from the cold. He was still shaking as he brushed the snow from his shoulders and approached the elevator bank. The doors to the middle elevator slid open just as he pressed the button, revealing Ian inside.

"Good morning," Ian said, glancing up from his phone.

Jay stepped inside and and waited for the doors to close, feeling self-conscious. "Good morning. The weather caused some delays on the metro."

"I imagine it did. The roads weren't much better."

The elevator began its ascent, and Jay was acutely aware of the confined space, smaller than he'd ever noticed before. Ian stood near the back corner, perfectly composed.

A subtle scent reached Jay, fresh and slightly spicy, rich without being overpowering. He'd never noticed Ian's cologne before, or perhaps he'd simply never paid attention. Now that he had, it seemed to fill the small space, making him hyperaware of Ian standing just a few centimeters away.

Jay looked at the floor numbers on the digital display, trying to ignore the way his pulse had quickened for no logical reason. This wasn't the first time they'd shared an elevator, though usually it was at the end of long days when they rode down together after finishing late work. But his morning, the moment felt different.

When the doors opened at their floor, Jay waited for Ian to exit first, then followed him through the office doors.

"Have a good morning, Jay."

"You too."

Jay walked to his desk, hanging his coat in the closet. He powered up his computer and reviewed Ian's schedule. The morning felt off-kilter, though he couldn't pinpoint why.

When he went to make his coffee, he was so distracted he nearly selected the wrong program, only realizing at the last moment he was about to brew an espresso instead of morning latte. Had he always been so aware of Ian? The scent, the sound of his voice in the small elevator space?

The questions felt foolish, and Jay pushed them aside as the day's workload demanded his attention. February had brought an avalanche of new client proposals that needed immediate review. He threw himself into the tasks, letting them take over his thoughts.

The hours passed quickly, consumed by phone calls, document preparation, and the endless stream of emails. Since the weather was anything but inviting and the workload had reached its winter peak, the team abandoned their lunchtime outings. Instead, they ordered takeout and ate in the office kitchen. Jay didn't join them, eating at his desk while composing messages between bites. He barely looked up from his screen all day.

By seven o'clock, most of the office had cleared out, but Ian remained at his desk, working through a complex analysis. Jay organized his own materials but made no move to leave.

"Jay," Ian said, appearing at his desk. "You don't need to stay late. I can handle the rest of this."

"I don't mind. There's still work to finish."

"I appreciate the dedication, but don't feel obligated to match my schedule."

"I want to help."

Ian returned to his office, and Jay continued working until Ian finally emerged at eight o'clock, briefcase in hand.

They rode the elevator down together. Jay noticed that scent again and had to resist the urge to breathe more deeply. The realization made him feel ridiculous—he was being weird about something as simple as cologne. He wondered what it was called, though asking would be presumptuous.

But it was pleasant. Comforting, echoing other moments when he had been close enough to Ian. When they had decorated the Christmas tree together, Ian reaching past him to hang ornaments. When Ian shook his hand, wishing him a happy New Year while they shared a glass of whiskey.

The elevator stopped at the ground floor, and Jay stepped out.

"Have a good evening, Ian."

"You too. Get some rest."

The elevator took Ian down to the underground garage where his car waited. Jay pushed through the lobby doors and stepped into the white world outside.

The snow had continued falling throughout the day, accumulating in thick drifts that softened every hard edge of the cityscape. The harsh sounds of traffic were muffled, creating an almost mystical tranquility. The streetlights created pools of golden illumination, making each flake visible as it drifted through the air. The city looked magical, like something from a postcard.

Jay remembered being seven years old, racing through snow while his mother called for him to come inside before he caught cold. Would he ever feel that pure joy again, that uncomplicated delight in winter weather? Or had the city permanently changed his relationship with snow, turning it into nothing more than an inconvenience that delayed trains and made commuting difficult?

The subway ran more smoothly on the return trip. When he reached his apartment, Jay still had energy to review materials for tomorrow's meetings. February was shaping up to be an intense month, with demands that would require his full attention.

He reheated his dinner and spread the papers across his small table, eating while reading through the reports. He worked for almost two hours. By the time he finished, his eyes ached from staring at numbers. As he got ready for bed, the memory of Ian's cologne lingered faintly in his mind. All he could do was hope that tomorrow would at least bring a normal metro schedule.

## Scene 2: New coat

Friday's temperatures seemed to bite through every layer of clothing. Jay pulled his old coat tighter as he hurried from the metro station, but the thin fabric did little to shield him from the arctic wind cutting between the buildings. The coat had served him sufficiently during previous winters, but those had been milder and with shorter commutes.

The metro had been delayed again, adding another twenty minutes to his commute. Jay stamped his feet in the elevator, trying to restore circulation as he rode up to the thirty-fourth floor.

The office provided blessed relief from the cold, but the atmosphere was tense with deadline pressure. Reports needed completion and the workday felt compressed and frantic.

"Anyone interested in that new sushi place for lunch?" Kim asked during the morning break.

"Are you insane?" Luc replied without looking up from his computer. "It's freezing out there."

"Plus we don't have time," Amy added. "These projections won't finish themselves."

Max appeared with a stack of takeout menus. "Lotus Kitchen delivers quickly, and their portions are decent. Should we just order in again?"

The team agreed with weary resignation. The nearby Lotus Kitchen had become their default choice because they delivered within thirty minutes and the portions were large enough to sustain them through long afternoons.

Jay ate his wok noodles at his desk while reviewing a tricky spreadsheet. The office felt like a bunker where they had all taken shelter from the hostile world outside.

As afternoon faded into evening, it became clear that the traditional Friday drinks would be another casualty of the weather.

"Drinks are canceled," Ela announced to no one in particular. "I'm going straight home before my fingers freeze off."

"Same," Luc agreed. "Maybe next week if this arctic nightmare ends."

Jay stayed until eight-thirty, helping Ian finish a complex client request. When they finally called it a day, the building felt almost empty, their footsteps echoing in the marble lobby.

Outside, the cold was unrelenting. Jay hunched into his inadequate coat and hurried toward the metro, each breath forming clouds that dissipated instantly in the harsh wind.

The evening train was delayed, leaving Jay standing on the platform in temperatures that seemed to seep through his bones. By the time he reached his apartment, he was shivering uncontrollably, his old coat having been utterly useless against the brutal weather.

His apartment felt like a sanctuary. The heating worked reliably, and the insulation blocked out the worst of the wind that rattled the windows. But the cold had sunk deep into his bones, and even the warm air couldn't immediately dispel it.

He went straight to the kitchen and put milk on the stove, stirring in honey as it warmed. The sweet, creamy drink helped chase away some of the chill, and he cupped the mug in both hands, letting the warmth spread through his fingers.

Saturday morning brought no relief from the cold, but Jay had a mission. His old coat simply wouldn't survive another week of this weather, and he refused to spend the rest of winter shivering through his commutes.

He took the metro to the shopping district, targeting the more upscale department stores where quality might actually match price. The first few shops yielded nothing suitable—either too expensive even by his new standards, or lacking the warmth he desperately needed.

In the fourth store he visited, he found a wool overcoat that immediately caught his attention. The fabric was thick and well-constructed, with a weight that promised real warmth. The cut was classic, appropriate for his work environment.

The price tag made him pause. It felt extravagant, even with his improved salary. His frugal instincts, developed through years of budgeting, rebelled against the expense.

But as he tried it on, feeling how the sleeves fell at exactly the right length, he forced himself to remember that he could afford this now. His position at Turner & Associates paid well, and winter clothing was a legitimate necessity.

"It looks very nice," the sales associate said. "That's one of our best winter coats. The wool is treated for water resistance, and the lining is designed for temperatures well below freezing."

The matching scarf added another amount to the total, but it completed the ensemble and provided additional warmth for his neck and face. Jay paid with his card, trying not to wince at the cost.

Walking home in his new coat, he felt warm. The wind that had been cutting through his old jacket now barely registered. The investment was painful but necessary.

Sunday was gray and gloomy, making the apartment feel cramped and dreary. Outside his window, the wind howled between buildings, sending snow spiraling in patterns that might have been beautiful under different circumstances. But the heavy overcast sky drained color from everything, making even the fresh snow look gray and uninviting.

Jay spread his work materials across his dining table, determined to make progress on the projects that had been pushed aside during the week's chaos. The workload seemed heavier than ever, with everything demanding attention and detailed analysis.

He worked through the morning, breaking only for a quick breakfast. The early darkness outside created an oppressive atmosphere, making it feel like evening by mid-afternoon. The sound of wind and blowing snow provided a constant backdrop that made concentration difficult.

Lunch was a brief interruption—soup heated on the stove while he continued reviewing documents. The warmth helped counter the psychological chill of the gray day outside.

By evening, Jay had made progress, but his eyes were tired from reading financial reports under artificial light. The weekend had been productive but joyless, another casualty of the relentless weather.

Winter was challenging . The cold was more severe, the commutes more difficult, and the workload more intense than anything he had previously experienced.

As he prepared for bed, Jay looked out his window at the snow-covered street below. The streetlights cast circles of light that illuminated the falling flakes, creating the same magical effect he had noticed earlier in the week. But tonight, the beauty felt alien and hostile.

## Scene 3: Company card

February continued with workload that made even the most experienced team members question their career choices. Jay learned that this was the season when Turner & Associates truly earned its reputation—the first quarter demanded everything from everyone, and the office turned into a war room. Jay started arriving early each morning, just in case the metro was delayed again. Despite his long days, the work on his desk only seemed to multiply.

The reflection in the elevator's polished steel had shown him someone who belonged here. The tailored gray suit he wore today was one of three he'd purchased recently, paired with a white shirt that perfectly fit his frame. The wool overcoat completed the look.

"Welcome to the battlefield," Amy said one morning, dropping a stack of files on Jay's desk. "Hope you got enough sleep in January."

The office atmosphere tensed noticeably. The sharp but pleasant concentration disappeared, replaced by a nervous bustle, punctuated by the sound of printers working overtime.

He soon learned that this frenzy had a name—strategy season. The phrase was repeated often enough for him to understand it was important, though its exact meaning remained elusive. Apparently, each client required a final summary document by mid-March, along with a detailed, labyrinthine analysis that could make or break the year's results.

Jay nodded along whenever someone mentioned it, piecing together fragments from overheard conversations. He knew it mattered, even if he couldn't quite explain how.

"Jay, do you have the analysis ready?" Ian called from his office.

"On your desk," Jay replied, not looking up from his screen. "I also pulled the comparative data."

Ian paused. "I didn't ask for comparative data."

"The numbers make more sense in context."

Ian smiled to himself. "Thank you."

Each day was busy, one spreadsheet after another. Jay stayed as late as Ian did, which meant most evenings stretched well past eight. Ian had stopped suggesting he leave earlier, a small victory that Jay treasured.

"Another late night?" Luc asked, passing Jay's desk with his coat already on.

"Seems that way."

"You know you don't have to prove anything anymore, right?"

"I'm not trying to prove anything. I just want to do the work well."

Luc watched him for a moment, then shrugged. "Fair enough. Don't burn out."

By Thursday of the second week of February, the strain was showing on everyone. Kim had snapped over a minor formatting issue. Even Max, typically unflappable, had muttered about early retirement while staring at his computer screen.

"Jay." Ian called from his office. "When you have a moment."

Jay gathered his tablet and the summary he was working on, stepping into the sanctuary of Ian's office.

"New client request came in," Ian said without looking up. "We'll need to restructure the Mayfield portfolio entirely. Can you block out the afternoon?"

"Of course." Jay made a note on his tablet. "Should I reschedule your three o'clock with the pension fund representatives?"

"Push it to tomorrow. This takes priority." Ian finally looked up, and Jay caught tension around his eyes. "It's going to be a long day."

"I'll let the team know we might need them to stay late again."

"They're going to mutiny. They're running on fumes. Make sure they at least eat something."

Jay had been thinking the same thing. Walking past the desks earlier, he'd noticed the slumped shoulders, the way conversations had dwindled only to necessary exchanges. The pizza and noodle orders were becoming the new norm, but morale was clearly suffering.

"Actually," Jay said, "I had a thought about that."

"Oh?"

"People are tired of the takeout options. What if we ordered from somewhere special tonight? Something that feels less like survival food."

Ian leaned back in his chair, considering. "That's not a terrible idea. Do you have somewhere in mind?"

"There's a new place in the Harbour District. They do elevated comfort food. It's expensive, but it shows we value their time."

Ian reached into his desk drawer and pulled out a company credit card, sliding it across the mahogany surface. "Use this for tonight. And going forward, actually. For situations like this."

Jay stared at the card. "Are you sure?"

"You should handle this kind of thing. I should have given you this weeks ago, but I assumed you'd take the promotion and transfer to the regular team."

The card felt unexpectedly heavy in Jay's palm.

"Use your discretion. Within reason, of course, but don't worry about the expense if it helps productivity. I should have thought of this myself."

Jay pocketed the card. "I'll take care of it."

Jay spent the next twenty minutes browsing the menu, keeping in mind team's preferences he learned over the months of daily lunches.

The delivery arrived at seven, filling the kitchen with aromas that made everyone stop what they were doing. Jay had ordered generously: herb-crusted salmon, braised short ribs, roasted chicken with vegetables, and an array of sides that could satisfy various dietary preferences. The dessert selection included individual chocolate tarts and lemon possets that looked like they belonged in a magazine.

"This is incredible," Zoe said, loading her plate. "Where did you find this place?"

"I've been keeping a list of restaurants that deliver to the financial district," Jay admitted. "Research for situations like this."

Amy raised an eyebrow. "You research restaurants for work?"

"I research everything for work."

The kitchen filled with laughter. Even Ian appeared, surveying the spread with what might have been surprise.

"This looks excellent," he said, accepting a plate from Jay. "Thank you for organizing it."

Jay watched Ian choose from the options, selecting the salmon and steamed vegetables—a deviation from his strict meal planning, but not a dramatic one. The sight of Ian joining the team in the kitchen, standing among them while they ate and talked, created a warmth that had nothing to do with the hot food.

"We should do this more often," Kim said around a bite of short ribs. "Not the working late part, but the good food part."

"I'll make sure we're prepared for the next busy stretch," Jay said.

Work was temporarily forgotten in favor of comparing favorite restaurants and debating the merits of various cuisines. Jay was drawn into discussions about the city's food scene, contributing insights he'd gathered from his exploration of the neighborhood.

When they finally returned to their desks, the atmosphere lightened. The work remained demanding, but the sense of being in it together had strengthened. Jay made a mental note to research team management strategies during his weekends. He wanted to understand how to support Ian more effectively in situations like this. For now, he was content with the knowledge that he'd made a small difference in his team's difficult day.

As the office gradually emptied, Jay remained at his desk, finishing the client summary that would be needed first thing tomorrow. Ian worked in his office, the glow of his screen visible through the partially open door.

## Scene 4: Planning

The pace of February left Jay with little mental capacity for anything beyond immediate work demands. Emails accumulated in his inbox faster than he could process them, and HR's stream of company announcements became background noise he barely registered.

When another message arrived with "Annual Company Retreat Update #3" in the subject line, Jay almost deleted it reflexively before remembering he should probably stay informed about major company events.

The email contained cheerful updates about the May retreat, complete with photos of the beachfront resort where they would spend three days. Pristine white sand stretched along turquoise water, while the hotel's amenities list read like something from a travel magazine—spa services, championship golf course, multiple restaurants, and evening entertainment that promised to be unforgettable.

Jay had submitted his application back in January when the first announcement arrived, filling out the basic form without giving it much thought. The retreat had seemed distant then, just another calendar entry to acknowledge and forget. But with HR's regular updates, the event kept surfacing in his consciousness like a recurring reminder of something he wasn't prepared to contemplate.

During the afternoon coffee break, he decided to ask his colleagues about their experiences with company retreats.

"I just read the retreat update. The place looks incredibly expensive."

"Oh, it is. Turner & Associates doesn't do anything halfway. You should see the budget they allocate for these things," Kim said.

Jay tried to imagine himself in such surroundings. "What exactly happens at these events?"

"Last year we went sailing. Instructors, fancy boats, the whole experience. The year before that was skiing in January. They actually flew us to a mountain lodge."

"And everyone goes?"

"Pretty much. It's not technically mandatory, but it's heavily encouraged. Plus we get Friday off, so it's basically a long weekend."

Amy joined the conversation. "The activities are actually fun. I know it sounds overwhelming, but they plan everything really well. Good food, interesting presentations, and enough free time to actually relax."

"What kind of activities are we talking about?"

"Beach volleyball, kayaking, wine tasting, cooking classes. They have a few options so you can pick what interests you. Trust me, after the winter we've had, three days of sunshine will feel like paradise."

Jay tried to picture himself participating in beach volleyball with senior executives and department heads. The image didn't come easily.

Luc wandered over, drawn by the conversation. "Are you worried about the retreat? It's not like the gala where you have to network and schmooze. It's much more casual."

"I'm just trying to wrap my head around the whole thing."

"The resort is ridiculous. But hey, if the company wants to spend money on pampering us, I'm not complaining."

"You'll love it," Ela assured him. "First company retreat is always special."

Jay managed a smile and returned to his desk, where another HR email waited with details about the April quarterly party. This announcement felt manageable—an evening event at a downtown venue, cocktails and dinner, nothing he hadn't handled before. After surviving the gala and various functions, a quarterly party seemed almost mundane.

"More corporate socializing," Ela commented, reading the same email. "At least this one won't have clients trying to corner us in hallways."

The reference to Cruver made Jay momentarily uncomfortable, but the feeling passed quickly. The quarterly party would be mostly internal staff, which eliminated most of his anxiety about the event.

Another HR email arrived later that week, this one about vacation planning. Jay opened it while eating his desk lunch, more wok noodles that had lost any appeal weeks ago.

HR requested that everyone submit their preferred vacation dates by the end of March, but when Jay checked the team calendar, Ian's time off requests weren't listed yet.

As Ian's assistant, he wasn't sure whether he should coordinate his time off to coincide with Ian's or arrange it for when Ian wouldn't be in the office. The question felt important enough to warrant clarification, but not urgent enough to interrupt Ian's current crisis management.

Jay decided to wait until Ian submitted his own plans, then match his timing accordingly. The decision could wait until their workload calmed down, whenever that might be. He marked the reminder for follow-up and returned to more pressing tasks.

Jay had no specific summer plans anyway. His previous vacations had been spent in his apartment, reading books he'd been meaning to finish and catching up on television series. Occasionally he would visit a museum or take walks through different neighborhoods, but nothing that required advance planning or major expenses.

The money he didn't spend on travel went directly into his emergency savings. Security mattered more than adventure, especially given his previous employment disasters. He liked knowing he had more than one safety valve in place—different accounts, different contingencies. One of them was even literal: a discreet envelope of cash tucked away where no one would think to look, his last line of defense if everything else failed.

But the company retreat was different. It was mandatory in all but name, a three-day commitment to being social and engaged with colleagues in an environment designed for relaxation but likely to be exhausting.

Jay tried to imagine himself at the beachfront resort, participating in group activities and evening entertainment, making conversation over elaborate dinners. The image felt foreign, like trying to picture himself in someone else's life.

The team was still at their desks on Friday evening, working through another late session fueled by takeout containers. The winter had fallen into a pattern of brutal cold and heavy workloads that seemed designed to test everyone's endurance.

"I'm starting to forget what sunlight looks like," Max complained, reviewing a complex investment analysis under the harsh fluorescent lights.

"May can't come fast enough," Amy agreed. "Three days at a beach resort is sounding better every day."

Jay continued organizing files while listening to his colleagues discuss the retreat with anticipation. Their enthusiasm was reassuring, even if he couldn't quite share it.

The retreat remained an abstract concept, something to worry about when the immediate pressures subsided. For now, February's demands consumed his thoughts, leaving little room for contemplating extravagant resorts.

Jay finished his tasks and prepared to face another frigid commute home, thankful for his new coat and the warm apartment that awaited him. Summer planning could wait until summer felt possible.

## Scene 5: Evening work

The office had emptied gradually throughout the evening, colleagues departing as their individual workloads permitted, calling out farewells that echoed in the gradually emptying space. Kim left first, followed by Ela, Luc and Zoe, then Max with a joke about "bankers' hours", and last Amy with a cheerful wave. The sounds of departure marked the transition from daytime to the quieter evening hours when real work could be accomplished.

Ian remained at his desk, reviewing materials that demanded his complete attention. Complex calculations required uninterrupted focus, something the bustling daytime office rarely provided. He preferred these evening sessions when he could think without constant interruptions.

Jay never announced his intention to stay late or asked whether his assistance was needed. He simply remained, transitioning seamlessly from daytime tasks to evening support.

The emails continued arriving even after business hours—urgent requests from clients, updates from overseas markets, messages that demanded immediate attention despite the late hour. Jay monitored Ian's inbox, flagging critical communications and sorting less urgent matters for morning review. His organizational system had evolved over the months, no longer requiring oversight.

When documents needed printing, Jay handled them without being asked. Contracts requiring signatures appeared on Ian's desk with relevant sections highlighted. The small post-it notes written in careful handwriting had become Jay's trademark, each containing exactly the information needed—reference numbers, modification details, deadline reminders.

"The Greenwell portfolio needs restructuring," Ian said, more to himself than to Jay.

"The last review flagged concerns about the tech sector allocation," Jay replied without looking up from his computer. "I pulled the performance data this morning. It's in the folder on your desk."

Ian retrieved the folder, finding exactly the information he needed. Jay had anticipated the requirement before Ian had fully articulated it, a development that had become increasingly common as the months passed.

"Thank you. Anything urgent in the evening emails?"

"Everest Capital wants to schedule a call for Monday morning. I've booked them at ten. Gelundis Fiduciary sent the documents you requested. I've printed them and placed them on your desk."

Jay's responses were always like this, succinct and comprehensive. Jay understood the flow of Ian's work, the priorities that mattered, the details that could wait. The learning curve had been steep initially, but now Jay functioned like an extension of Ian's own organizational system.

Ian noticed Jay's eyes drift to his empty coffee cup, saw the slight furrow of concern that appeared when Ian worked too long without a break. Jay never presumed to offer refreshments or suggest rest, but his worry was evident in his expression. But Ian made a point of refilling his own coffee. Jay was his assistant, not his servant, and Ian refused to blur that distinction regardless of Jay's obvious willingness to help with anything requested.

For years, working late had been a solitary grind, necessary but joyless, sustained by determination. The office became a tomb after hours. Now, Jay changed the atmosphere. He provided something Ian hadn't realized he was missing—silent, supportive companionship. He moved through the office quietly, never interrupting Ian's concentration but always being available when assistance was needed. He delivered documents during Ian's natural pause points and seemed to sense when Ian needed space to think. Jay never complained about the long hours, never seemed impatient to leave, never made Ian feel guilty for the demanding schedule.

But the guilt remained. Jay's dedication seemed boundless, his willingness to stay overtime and handle demanding tasks both impressive and troubling. Ian wondered whether he was taking advantage of Jay's eagerness to please, whether the young man felt pressured to exceed normal expectations which wasn't fair or sustainable.

Yet Ian couldn't bring himself to send Jay home earlier. The Friday evening stretched, and Ian extended their work sessions simply to postpone the return to his empty penthouse.

"Ian. And Jay." Leo's voice interrupted Ian's thoughts as his friend appeared in the office doorway. His visits had become less frequent since the new year chaos began—perhaps his mysterious schedule had intensified as well.

Jay looked up from his computer, acknowledging Leo with a polite nod. Without a word, he rose from his desk and walked toward the kitchen area, clearly intending to give them privacy for their conversation.

Ian watched Jay's retreat with amusement. Over the months, Jay had developed an obvious wariness toward Leo that manifested in blatant avoidance. Jay remained courteous, but he made no effort to encourage casual interaction and seemed to take every opportunity to remove himself from Leo's vicinity.

"Your assistant is remarkably discreet," Leo observed, sitting on the chair across from Ian's desk.

"He is."

"And devoted."

Ian's expression hardened. "If you have something specific to discuss, please get to the point."

"You have to admit, the arrangement is working splendidly. You look more relaxed than I've seen you in years."

"The workload requires extended hours. Jay understands that."

"Does he? Or does he simply enjoy spending time with his boss? Either way, at least you're not working yourself to death in solitary misery anymore. When work becomes indistinguishable from leisure, one can hardly call it a hardship."

"That's enough," Ian said sharply. "You're reading entirely too much into it."

"I'm just pleased to see you looking less exhausted than you did six months ago."

Ian wanted to argue, to deny Leo's implications, but he was unable to formulate a convincing rebuttal. It was true that work had become more enjoyable since Jay's arrival. Even during those early months when suspicion coloured every interaction, the dynamic had felt invigorating.

His initial reservations about Jay now seemed not just unfounded but actively foolish. Jay's shyness, his obvious intimidation by the office's grandeur and his colleagues' credentials, his almost painful dedication to performing every task perfectly—all of it was disarming, making suspicion impossible to maintain.

Ian had watched obsessively during those first weeks, cataloguing every interaction and analysing every response. But Jay's awkwardness was too genuine to manufacture, his gratitude too heartfelt to be fake. The young man's reactions to praise, his visible relief when tasks were completed successfully—none of it could be performed that convincingly.

"He's competent," Ian said, unwilling to elaborate further.

"He's more than competent. He's exactly what you needed. Just remember to occasionally acknowledge that fact. Even perfect assistants require recognition."

After Leo left, Jay returned from the kitchen, sitting back at his desk without comment or question about the conversation he had deliberately avoided.

Ian continued working, acutely aware of the way Jay turned what should have been tedious late-evening drudgery into tender contentment. Jay was, by any reasonable measure, a gem—rare, valuable, and deserving of careful handling. The question was whether Ian was providing the appreciation he warranted, or whether he was simply taking advantage of Jay's generous nature.

Jay had become an integral part of Ian's daily life, which Ian was only starting to fully recognize. The realization was both comforting and concerning. Ian did his best to keep emotional distance, but Jay was undermining those defences without Ian quite understanding how or when it had happened.
