# Chapter 9: New Year

## Scene 1: After the holidays

The office felt different after the holidays, with most of the desks empty and the usual bustle absent. Jay arrived earlier than necessary, energized and eager.

"Good morning," Jay said as he passed Ian's office, surprised that his boss was already at his desk.

Ian looked up from his computer. "Good morning, Jay. I trust you had a pleasant holiday?"

"Very pleasant, thank you. Quiet and restful." Jay hesitated in the doorway. "How was your Christmas?"

"Productive." Ian's tone offered no invitation for further inquiry. "I've prepared a list of priority items for this week. With our reduced staff, we'll need to focus on essential tasks."

"Of course. I'm ready."

Jay sat at his desk with renewed purpose. The break had done him good. He felt clear-headed, approaching each task with focus.

Only a skeleton crew remained during the week between Christmas and New Year's, and even that had been reduced when Ela called in sick with the flu that morning, her voice hoarse and apologetic over the phone.

"Don't worry about it," Ian had told her. "Rest and recover properly. We'll manage."

Luc arrived an hour later, looking considerably less energetic. "This place is like a ghost town."

"Ela called in sick this morning—caught the flu." Jay replied.

"So it's just the three of us? This should be interesting."

The morning passed smoothly despite their reduced numbers. Jay worked through client correspondence while Ian handled the more complex enquiries.

Luc emerged from the open space looking slightly overwhelmed. "Thank God you're both here. I've got three client reports that need to be finished by Friday, and Ela was supposed to handle most of them."

"I can take on the market analysis," Jay offered immediately. "I've been studying the proper formats."

Ian watched Jay's eager expression and felt a tightness in his chest. The young man's recovery was remarkable—he'd regained that bright, determined look that had initially caught Ian's attention. But Ian's own reaction to that enthusiasm now felt dangerous.

"That would be great," Ian said. "I'll review the parameters with you."

Jay worked diligently, asking questions and tackling complex data into clear reports. Ian fought the urge to watch him too closely.

Every instinct screamed at Ian to maintain distance. The dream from the night before had left him wary, hyperaware of every casual interaction. But he also recognized that Jay's confidence remained fragile despite his improved mood. One careless gesture could undo months of progress. So Ian forced himself to be patient and accommodating, answering Jay's questions thoroughly. The effort was exhausting—like walking a tightrope while pretending everything was normal.

By lunchtime, it became clear that the workload would be manageable but demanding. Jay volunteered to take on additional responsibilities, preparing reports that would typically be distributed among the full team.

By late afternoon, Luc had finished his assignments and was preparing to leave. "I'm heading out," he announced, gathering his things. "My girlfriend's cooking dinner, and I don't want to be late."

"Have a good evening," Ian replied, not looking up from his computer.

"Don't work too hard, you two. The world won't end if something waits until tomorrow."

As Luc's footsteps faded down the corridor, Jay glanced at the remaining work on his desk. His analysis was nearly complete, but Ian still had several client summaries to review.

"I can stay late and help with those," Jay said, gesturing toward Ian's stack of documents. "It's not like I have anywhere else to be."

"That's not necessary," Ian replied quickly. "I can manage it."

"Please, let me help. I want to contribute."

Ian looked at Jay's earnest expression and felt his resolve waver. He could see the importance this held for Jay—the chance to prove himself useful, to be trusted with meaningful work. Refusing would likely hurt Jay's feelings.

"All right," Ian said finally.

The next few hours passed quickly. They worked in silence, Jay at his desk while Ian remained in his office. The building grew quieter as everyone else left and the cleaning crew finished their rounds.

Jay had stayed late before, but almost always with other people present. The current situation felt different—since no one else was there, it felt more intimate. The warm glow of the Christmas lights in the corridor cast everything in a softer light.

Jay worked through a stack of client summaries, but he couldn't shake the awareness of Ian across the room. They were alone together like never before—no colleagues nearby, no interruptions from the office activity.

"Jay." Ian's voice made him look up. "Could you review these projections? I want to ensure the calculations are accurate before I submit them."

Jay accepted the documents, their fingers brushing briefly as the papers changed hands. The contact lasted only a moment, but Jay felt his skin burn at the touch.

"Of course," he said, hoping his voice sounded steadier than he felt.

As Jay worked through the projections, he was acutely aware of Ian returning to his desk, the sound of his chair, the rustle of papers being organized. The office felt simultaneously vast and cramped, as if they were occupying a private space carved out from the larger corporate world.

Jay discovered something unexpected as the evening progressed: he trusted Ian. Not just as a supervisor, but as a man. The realization struck him as he worked through the data—he felt entirely safe with Ian, protected by his integrity.

Under normal circumstances, being alone in an office with a senior manager would have made him anxious. His previous experience had taught him to be wary of men in positions of power, to expect the worst from such situations. But with Ian, that fear simply didn't exist. Jay couldn't explain the certainty he felt about Ian's character—they didn't know each other well, they had shared few personal conversations. Yet Jay trusted him implicitly.

The trust was absolute and unquestioning, a feeling Jay hadn't experienced with anyone in years. Ian could ask him to do anything, and Jay would comply without hesitation, confident that the request would be reasonable and appropriate.

This certainty should have been comforting, but instead it made Jay's pulse quicken. He trusted Ian not to take advantage, not to make him uncomfortable, not to use his position inappropriately. It was a level of faith Jay hadn't experienced since starting his career. But the intimacy of the moment also made his skin tingle. The quiet office, the warm lighting, the sense of being alone together—it created an atmosphere that felt charged, even though Jay knew nothing would happen.

Jay forced himself to focus on the documents, but his awareness of Ian remained constant. Through the windows, the city lights twinkled like earthbound stars, and Jay felt as though they were suspended in a moment outside of normal time.

He glanced up to find Ian watching him with an unreadable expression. For a heartbeat, neither man looked away, then Ian returned to his work, and Jay did the same. But the air in the office vibrated with tension that Jay couldn't identify.

## Scene 2: Before New Year

As the week progressed, Jay threw himself into his work with determination, driven by a desire to make Ian's workload as manageable as possible. After nearly five months at Turner & Associates, he'd developed some expertise in the company's basic operations and client needs.

His evenings at home had been spent studying client portfolios and industry standards, and the investment was paying off. Jay could now manage simple reports and handle standard client correspondence with minimal supervision. He was reserving only the most crucial assignments for Ian.

"You're a godsend," Luc said on Wednesday morning, watching Jay organize the day's priorities. "Without you, I'd probably be sleeping in the office."

Ian observed Jay's initiative, not just in organization but in the matters that drove their business. His reports were clear and his instincts increasingly sound.

Jay stayed late every evening, working through tasks that would have otherwise fallen to Ian. One evening, as the office began to empty out, Ian paused by Jay's desk.

"It's the holiday season, Jay. You should head home and get some rest."

"When I get home, I study the work materials anyway," Jay said, not looking up from his computer. "It doesn't matter to me if I'm in the office a bit longer."

Ian shook his head in resignation. He recognized the pattern—the complete absorption in work that left no room for personal life. He didn't want Jay to become like him, a monk married to the job, but he was hardly in a position to criticize.

The lunch routine changed organically. With most of the team gone, Jay and Luc abandoned the group outings to restaurants. The weather was brutal, and neither felt motivated to venture outside for extended periods.

"I grabbed sandwiches from that bakery nearby," Jay announced, holding up a paper bag. "Turkey club and roast beef. Take your pick."

"Lifesaver," Luc replied, accepting the turkey club. "I was dreading another frozen walk to find food."

They ate in the office kitchen, a space Jay had rarely used for eating before. Ian appeared at his usual lunch hour, retrieving a container from the refrigerator and heating it in the microwave.

"Mind if I join you?" Ian asked, though he was already sitting down at the small table.

"Of course," Jay said, but he felt suddenly self-conscious about his lunch.

The conversation was stilted at first. Ian was private even during breaks, never discussing personal matters or revealing much about his life outside the office. But Jay enjoyed seeing this different side of his boss—less formal, more human.

"What does Ian eat?" Jay asked on Luc, realizing he'd never paid attention to his boss's meal habits.

"Ian has a personal meal service. Everything prepared according to optimal macros and delivered fresh."

Jay looked up, surprised. "Seriously? I thought he just skipped meals."

Luc laughed. "Does he look like someone who skips meals? He's a gym rat. Working out is basically the only thing he does besides work. Very disciplined about it."

Jay took in the information with interest. He'd never thought about Ian's life outside the office, but now he watchem him more carefully. It was entirely possible that he was actually quite muscular beneath those tailored clothes. The broad shoulders, the way he held himself—it all supported Luc's observation.

Jay also identified the sleek containers in the office refrigerator. Each meal was portioned exactly, the contents clearly optimized for nutrition.

As New Year's Eve approached, the three of them worked closer than ever. Several last-minute client emergencies tested their coordination, but they handled each situation smoothly. Jay felt his confidence soaring with each successful resolution.

The work was meaningful, bringing him satisfaction unlike anything he'd experienced before. He began to understand why some people became workaholics—there was something deeply fulfilling about performing duties to the highest standard and seeing tangible results.

Jay looked toward Ian's office and felt a new understanding of his boss. Perhaps Ian felt this same fulfillment, this sense of purpose that made long hours feel worthwhile.

The last day before New Year was notably calmer. Client correspondence had slowed to a trickle, and their major assignments were complete. The entire financial district switched into celebratory mode.

Around four o'clock, Ian surprised both Jay and Luc by appearing at their desks.

"Would you join me in my office?"

They followed him, exchanging curious glances. Ian moved to the credenza where he kept a selection of glasses and a bottle of whiskey Jay had never noticed before.

"I thought we should toast the year," Ian said, pouring three generous measures. "It's been particularly successful."

Luc accepted his glass eagerly, but Jay hesitated. "I'm not sure this is entirely appropriate—"

Ian laughed, a sound that softened his entire demeanor. "We're done with our work, and we'll all be leaving momentarily. Even I won't stay late today. If it were truly improper, I wouldn't keep alcohol and glasses in my office in the first place."

Jay accepted his glass, still uncertain but charmed by Ian's unexpected warmth. He had never seen Ian laugh this way—not the polite smile, but genuine joy. He had a feeling this moment would stay with him for a long time.

"To a successful year," Ian said, raising his glass.

"To surviving the holidays," Luc added with a grin.

They drank, and Luc immediately looked impressed. "This is excellent whiskey."

"It's actually my best bottle," Ian admitted.

Luc went pale. "How best are we talking?"

"Best enough that you should savor it, not gulp it."

Jay sipped his whiskey and immediately made a face. "I never understood hard liquor."

Both men laughed at his expression, and Jay felt a warm thread of connection despite his distaste for the drink. He persevered, drinking stubbornly while continuing to grimace.

"It's an acquired taste," Ian said. "Wine is more forgiving for beginners."

"What do you usually drink?" Jay asked, emboldened by the alcohol and the relaxed atmosphere.

"Scotch, mostly. Sometimes bourbon. I prefer aged spirits—they have more complexity."

Ian explained the differences between various whiskeys, his passion for the subject evident in his detailed descriptions. Jay listened intently, fascinated by this glimpse into Ian's personal preferences.

The conversation continued for another twenty minutes, all three men feeling closer after the intense week they'd shared. Even Ian was more at ease, the initial tension from his dream having subsided. He'd regained confidence in his ability to compartmentalize his attraction.

Jay radiated happiness. The innocence in his expression touched something in Ian's chest, a warmth he didn't dare examine too closely.

When their drinks were finished, Ian got up, offering a brief farewell. "Happy New Year, Jay," Ian said, extending his hand. "Thank you for everything this week."

Jay shook his hand, looking a little embarrassed. "Happy New Year, Ian. I'm glad I could be useful."

Jay left the office that evening feeling fulfilled and secure. Despite everything, this had been a good year. Challenging and not free from drama, but ultimately strengthening.

He felt truly blessed, and even his initial anger at Leo had faded. The man's meddling had actually resulted in something wonderful.

On impulse, Jay sent a message to Leo's company phone: "Happy New Year. You are forgiven."

Across town at an upscale party, Leo read the message and grinned. Combined with Ian's recent tennis-related tantrum, things were clearly progressing in the right direction.

Jay spent New Year's Eve alone in his apartment, watching fireworks bloom across the city skyline at midnight. He felt good—genuinely, deeply good.

In his own apartment, Ian stood at his window watching the same fireworks. He felt a similar contentment, a sense that the year had brought positive change.

Both men thought about each other as the new year began, and for once, these thoughts were warm and hopeful, not complicated by doubt or fear.

## Scene 3: Bonus

The first week of January brought Turner & Associates back to life at full throttle, the year-start chaos intensified by the dual demands of annual and quarterly reports. Ela was still home recovering from the flu, Kim was on vacation for another week, and the smaller team meant no one had time to ease into the new year. Jay arrived earlier than usual on his first day back, only to find Ian already in his office with the door ajar, a half-empty coffee cup beside him and several files open on his desk.

"Good morning," Jay said as he passed.

Ian glanced up from a document. "Good morning. I trust you had a good holiday?"

"I did, thank you. I hope yours was restful."

"It was," Ian replied, before turning back to his work.

Jay's tasks felt comfortable now, second nature after months of practice. At eleven-thirty, his computer chimed with a new email. The subject line read: "Year-End Performance and Bonus Announcement." Jay opened it immediately.

"Team,

I want to congratulate everyone on an exceptional year. Turner & Associates has achieved record-breaking performance across all sectors, exceeding our targets by significant margins. This success is a result of your dedication, expertise, and collaborative efforts.

Year-end bonuses have been processed and will appear in your accounts shortly. These amounts reflect not only our financial success but also your individual contributions to our achievements.

I look forward to building on this momentum in the coming year.

Best regards, Ian Hagen Chief Financial Officer"

Jay read the message, then checked his phone for bank notifications. Nothing yet. He wondered what constituted a bonus at a firm like Turner & Associates. His previous job had given modest holiday bonuses—enough for a nice dinner or a small gift at best. Other firms had weeks of waiting, sometimes months, for accounting to finalize numbers and senior partners to approve distributions. It felt like that could only be explained by Ian's standards, applied to himself before anyone else.

He tried to focus on his work, but kept checking his phone every few minutes. Around noon, the notification finally arrived.

Jay stared at the screen, dumbfounded. The number couldn't be right. Eighty thousand. For a second, he thought it had to be eight thousand, something generous but still plausible. He counted the digits again: one, two, three, four, five. Five digits. More money than he'd ever seen in his account at once.

He stood abruptly, then sat back down. This amount could change everything. In a couple of months, he could make a down payment on an apartment—not just any apartment, but a decent one in a good neighbourhood. The possibility felt surreal.

Jay walked quickly to Luc's desk, where his colleague was typing with unusual enthusiasm.

"Luc, could I ask you something?"

Luc looked up, grinning. "Let me guess. You got your bonus notification and think there's been a mistake."

"Well... yes. There has to be an error."

"No error. Welcome to the real benefits of working in big finance. When the year is good, the bonus is mind-blowing. And this year was spectacular."

Jay glanced around the office. Max was on the phone, gesturing excitedly as he spoke. "I'm thinking the Maldives this time," he was saying. "Maybe that resort with the overwater bungalows." Zoe sat at her desk with a grin, apparently running numbers for apartment searches.

Amy walked past with a stack of files, looking pleased. "Congratulations, Jay," she said. "First bonus at Turner & Associates?"

"Yes, and apparently last, because I think I'm having a heart attack."

She laughed. "You'll get used to it. Though I hope you never take it for granted."

"I don't think that's possible," Jay said.

"So what's the plan? New apartment? Fancy car? Month-long vacation?" Luc asked.

"I honestly don't know. I never expected anything like this."

"Well, you earned it. Ian doesn't give bonuses out of charity."

Jay felt a warm flush of pride. "It still feels unreal."

"Enjoy it," Luc said. "You've been working hard. You deserve to celebrate."

Jay returned to his desk, still processing the morning's revelation. He opened his laptop and began researching apartment listings, then stopped himself. Moving felt premature. This windfall could disappear as quickly as it had appeared. Markets fluctuated, jobs ended, and he'd learned not to trust good fortune too completely.

But he could make some smaller improvements. His wardrobe, for instance. He'd been wearing the same rotation of modest clothes for months, carefully maintaining them to extend their life. His colleagues dressed impeccably, and while no one had ever commented on his appearance, he sometimes felt self-conscious about the gap in quality.

Jay made a decision. He would upgrade his work wardrobe—nothing extravagant, but a definite improvement. Three new suits, properly tailored, that would help him feel more confident.

After lunch, he researched tailors in the city centre. He found a well-reviewed boutique within walking distance that specialized in business attire. It wasn't a place that built suits from scratch. Most of their pieces were ready-made, adjusted to fit each client, but that suited him just fine. It was a world apart from the department-store racks he'd relied on so far.

The fittings could be done in a single visit, with alterations ready in about a week. The prices were reasonable, especially considering his unexpected financial cushion. He decided not to stay late that day and to visit it immediately, suspecting the workload in the coming days would be even more unforgiving, and doubting he would have the time.

The boutique occupied a narrow storefront. Inside, the atmosphere was quiet, with racks of suits in rich fabrics and a small seating area near the fitting rooms.

A middle-aged man approached as Jay entered. "Good afternoon. How can I help you?"

"I'm looking for three business suits," Jay said. "Nothing too flashy, but well-made."

The man nodded approvingly. "Excellent choice. Quality pieces that will serve you well. Let me show you some options."

For the next hour, Jay tried on suits in navy, gray, and a subtle pinstripe. The tailor took measurements, discussing fabric choices and fit preferences. Jay selected conservative styles that would complement his existing wardrobe while elevating his overall appearance.

"These will be ready in ten days," the tailor said as Jay completed his payment. "You'll find the improvement in confidence is worth the investment."

Walking to the metro, he felt good. Finally he wouldn't worry about standing out for the wrong reasons. The suits wouldn't make him equal to his colleagues' backgrounds or education, but they would eliminate one source of self-consciousness. The new year had begun with unexpected generosity, and Jay was determined to make the most of it.

## Scene 4: Friday evening reflections

The days that followed were relentless. New projects launched alongside the backlog from the break, and with both the quarterly and annual reports due, there was little room to breathe. Ian was at his desk before Jay arrived each morning and still there after he left in the evenings. Jay's own workload felt heavier than December's, each task flowing into the next without pause. Lunch hours vanished, everyone ate at their desks, picking at takeout while answering emails. The printers ran constantly, conference calls overlapped.

Eventually Ela returned from her sick leave with dramatic stories about her recovery and Kim came back from his extended vacation with a deep tan and a collection of choice observations about tropical tourist destinations. The office felt complete again.

Despite the workload, the end-of-year gala dominated conversations throughout the building. Jay overheard snippets of planning discussions everywhere—in the elevator, during coffee breaks, over lunch. The venue this year was apparently the Grand Hotel, with its famous ballroom. Amy mentioned shopping for a new dress, while Max debated between his black tuxedo and charcoal formal wear.

"Have you picked up your tuxedo yet?" Zoe asked Jay during a brief break between meetings.

"Not yet," Jay said. "I should probably collect it this weekend."

The prospect of attending such an elegant gathering still felt surreal, but his growing confidence made it seem less intimidating than it once would have.

By the end of the week, the quarterly reports were finished. The pace eased marginally, though the annual reports and early-year planning kept everyone busy. Still, Monday brought the return of the team's lunch outings. The weather was bitter, but the change of scenery was welcome. The pasta place, the sushi bar, the bistro with its onion soup, and Jay's favourite Willow & Sage were all back in rotation.

"The weather's brutal," Luc said during lunch, warming his hands around a cup of coffee. "I'm ready for spring already."

"At least it's not snowing," Amy replied. "Remember last January? We were practically trapped in the office for two weeks."

"That was actually productive," Max said. "Nothing like a blizzard to eliminate distractions."

Jay listened to the banter, appreciating how naturally he'd become part of these conversations. The awkwardness of his early weeks felt like a distant memory.

Friday afternoon brought a sense of completion as the week's projects wrapped up. Jay finished organizing Ian's calendar for the following week and compiled the status reports that would be distributed Monday morning. The office gradually emptied as people prepared for the weekend.

"Hey Jay," Luc said, stopping by his desk as he gathered his coat. "Feel like grabbing drinks? There's a new place downtown that's supposed to have decent craft beer."

Jay looked up from his computer, considering the invitation. Luc had been asking regularly, and Jay had consistently declined with polite but vague excuses.

"Thanks, but I'll pass," Jay said. "I appreciate the invitation though."

"Come on, it's Friday. Live a little."

Jay paused, then decided to be honest. "Pubs overwhelm me, to be completely frank. I'd rather avoid them if possible."

Luc raised an eyebrow, then grinned. "Fair enough. Though you realize you'll still have to attend on special occasions, right? Work celebrations, that sort of thing."

"I suppose I'll manage when necessary," Jay said with a slight smile.

"We'll ease you into it gradually. Start with quiet wine bars, work our way up to actual chaos."

Jay laughed. "I'll keep that in mind."

After Luc left, Jay finished his remaining tasks and prepared to leave. The office felt peaceful in the early evening light, most desks empty except for the occasional colleague working late. Ian's office light was still on, but he decided not to disturb him.

Since the team had returned to full strength and the quarterly reports were done, Jay no longer needed to stay late every day. The consistent overtime of December had become unnecessary, leaving him with free afternoons. Strangely, he missed those quiet hours when the office emptied and he could focus completely on his work while Ian handled urgent matters in the adjacent office.

Jay collected his coat and headed home, stepping into the crisp January air. The city felt different in winter. People moved with purpose, bundled against the cold but undeterred by it.

Back at his apartment, Jay hung up his coat and surveyed the space. The small Christmas tree still stood in the corner, though he'd planned to pack it away. It felt reassuring, a reminder of how much had changed in the past months.

He made dinner and ate while reviewing the day's events. The routine of office life had become so natural that he sometimes forgot how foreign it had once seemed.

As evening deepened, Jay got restless. He cleaned up the dinner dishes, then finally dismantled the Christmas tree, packing away the ornaments for next year. The task felt both melancholy and hopeful, marking the end of one chapter and the beginning of another.

He sat by the window with a cup of tea, watching the city lights emerge in the darkness. Usually, he enjoyed these quiet moments in his apartment, the solitude providing a necessary counterbalance to the day's social interactions. Tonight, however, the silence felt different—not peaceful, but empty.

Jay wondered if Ian was still at the office. It was past eight o'clock, late even by Ian's standards, but not unprecedented. The man seemed to find comfort in work, using it as both challenge and refuge. Jay understood the appeal, having experienced it himself during the busiest weeks of December.

The thought of Ian working alone in the office stirred something in Jay's chest—not pity, but a kind of sympathetic understanding. They shared a preference for solitude, but perhaps that preference masked a long buried need for connection.

Jay considered returning to the office, ostensibly to collect something he'd forgotten, but dismissed the idea as transparent. Ian valued his privacy, and Jay respected that.

Instead, he thought about gym memberships. The idea had never appealed to him before—he'd always preferred leisurely activities like reading or walking. But knowing that Ian enjoyed physical exercise made the prospect more interesting. Not because he wanted to impress Ian, but because he was curious about experiences that brought Ian fulfilment.

Jay opened his laptop and researched fitness centres in his neighbourhood. The variety was overwhelming—yoga studios, boxing gyms, traditional fitness centres with pools and classes. He bookmarked several options, planning to visit them over the weekend.

The evening stretched ahead of him. Jay sat deeper into his chair, accepting the unusual restlessness as part of his continuing evolution. Six months ago, he would have treasured this solitude without question. Now, he wondered what else might be possible, what other activities he might enjoy if he remained open to them.

## Scene 5: Preparations

The week before the gala, Jay made his rounds to collect the various elements of his formal ensemble. The tailor greeted him politely, clearly pleased with the final result.

"Perfect fit," the man said as Jay examined himself in the fitting room mirror. "You have good proportions for formal wear."

Jay turned slowly, assessing the reflection. The tuxedo looked good on him. The black wool draped smoothly across his shoulders, the trousers fell in clean lines, and the overall effect was both elegant and understated. He looked like he just might belong at the Grand Hotel.

"The shoes as well," the tailor continued, presenting a pair of patent leather oxfords. "Premium leather, hand-stitched. They'll be comfortable for an entire evening."

Jay tried them on, walking across the fitting room floor. The shoes felt substantial but not heavy, with the quality that would last for years.It wasn't cheap, but looking at the complete ensemble, he felt the investment was justified.

"I'm satisfied," Jay said, changing back into his regular clothes. "Thank you for the excellent work."

"You'll want to practice that a few times before the event," the shop assistant advised, demonstrating the bow tie technique. "Most people underestimate how tricky it can be under pressure."

Walking back to his apartment, Jay realized he needed to address another detail: his hair. While he kept it short and neat, the gala demanded a more refined appearance. He didn't want to embarrass himself or, by extension, his colleagues with a casual look at such a prestigious event.

Finding a hairdresser was more challenging than expected. Jay called several salons near his apartment, only to discover they were completely booked. The third salon he contacted explained that January was peak season for formal events, with many people scheduling appointments after the holidays.

"I'm sorry, but we're fully booked through next Friday," the receptionist said. "You might try calling some of the higher-end establishments. They sometimes have last-minute availability."

Jay's anxiety grew as he worked through his list. He'd assumed a simple haircut would be easy to arrange, but his procrastination had created an unnecessary obstacle. Finally, a salon in the upscale shopping district had an opening. Jay winced at the price but accepted. The amount would have been unthinkable a month ago, but his bonus had changed his perspective on necessary expenses. Looking presentable at the gala was worth the money.

Thursday afternoon, Jay collected his new suits from the boutique. The tailor had outdone himself—each piece fit impeccably, turning Jay's appearance from merely acceptable to elegant. The gray suit matched out his eyes, the navy complemented his pale complexion, and the pinstripe added sophistication to his overall look.

"These will serve you well. Quality pieces that will last for years with proper care," the tailor said.

Jay thanked him and headed to the nearby department store to complete his wardrobe upgrade. He selected several shirts in fine cotton, with better cuts and details than his previous purchases. The prices matched the quality—each shirt cost more than he'd previously spent on an entire outfit—but the improvement was undeniable.

Returning home, Jay faced an unexpected problem. His small studio apartment had exactly one closet, and it was now overwhelmed by his expanded wardrobe. The new suits, shirts, and tuxedo required more space than he had available.

The old clothes would have to go. Jay sorted through his existing wardrobe, identifying pieces that were worn beyond repair or simply outclassed by his new acquisitions. Several shirts with frayed cuffs joined the donation pile, along with sweaters that had seen better days and casual wear that no longer fit his image.

The hardest decision involved his first suit—a basic gray piece he'd bought for job interviews years ago. The fabric was inferior to his new purchases, the cut unflattering in retrospect, but it held sentimental value. Jay held it up, remembering his nervousness during those early interviews, the hope and uncertainty that had accompanied each job application.

After a moment's hesitation, he folded it and placed it on the donation pile. His small space couldn't accommodate keeping clothes for purely sentimental reasons.

The reorganized closet looked dramatically different. Where once hung a collection of serviceable but uninspiring garments, now stood a selection of elegant pieces. It looked almost comical in his modest studio apartment—these expensive clothes seemed to belong in a larger space, with better lighting and more sophisticated furniture.

Jay purchased a set of cedar sachets to keep his clothes fresh, placing them strategically throughout the closet. The small apartment sometimes retained cooking odours or general staleness, and he refused to let his investment in quality clothing be compromised by poor storage conditions.

As he organized his new wardrobe, Jay thought about other aspects of his appearance. He had never worn cologne, partly from lack of interest but mostly because of cost. Now, with his improved financial situation, the option seemed worth considering.

He researched fragrances online, quickly becoming discouraged by the complexity of the topic. Base notes, top notes, seasonal appropriateness, projection, longevity—the vocabulary alone was intimidating. Jay imagined himself smelling like a department store fragrance counter. Better to smell neutral than to risk olfactory offence, especially at his first major company event. The last thing Jay wanted was to arrive at the gala smelling like an overzealous teenager.

Jay stepped back and surveyed his apartment with fresh eyes. The studio was small—a single bed, a compact living area, a kitchenette with a small dining table that served his needs. The space was efficiently organized, everything in its proper place.

He had always found the apartment cosy. The size matched his lifestyle and budget. Now, with his closet full of expensive clothes and his bank account containing an unprecedented amount of money, he could afford something larger. The thought had crossed his mind several times over the past weeks.

But Jay dismissed the idea each time it arose. His current apartment felt secure and manageable. Moving would require faith in his continued employment and success—faith he wasn't quite ready to embrace. The studio represented stability and caution, qualities that had served him well throughout his life.

Besides, he enjoyed the space. The large windows provided good natural light and the neighbourhood was beautiful and safe. Jay sat on the sofa with a cup of tea, pleased with his preparations for the gala. The tuxedo was perfect and the hairdresser appointment was scheduled. He felt ready to represent Turner & Associates with dignity.
