# Chapter 49: Shelter

## Scene 1: The Correct Course

The clock read 04:50. Ian had been awake since three, and before that he had slept in fragments. At a quarter past four he had stopped pretending the bed would serve any purpose. Instead he had sat in the dark and reviewed evidence.

It was an ugly word for it. Evidence belonged to fraud, litigation, regulatory review. Evidence was what one assembled when the truth had to be made undeniable in front of people who refused to see it. He had no tribunal here. No opponent. No one to persuade but himself, and he was proving to be a difficult audience.

The evidence was impossible to dismiss any longer.

Jay looking at him across the island breakfast table as if the morning had been invented for the two of them alone. Jay on the deck of that ridiculous cruise ship, pressed against Ian's shoulder, not for balance but because he had wanted to remain there. Jay's hand in his, accepted day after day. Jay in the hot springs suite, drunk and miserable, asking to be held. Jay at five thirty with his eyes on Ian's door.

Then there were the blushes. Jay blushed easily and about many things, that had always been true, but Ian had learned the difference between the blush that came from embarrassment and a different kind. A blush at his hand on Jay's back in the gym. Another when he touched Jay's wrist. A blush followed by a long silence in the sauna, eyes sliding away and returning despite Jay's obvious effort to behave like a rational adult. The invented cold the next day, so transparent that only Ian's own cowardice had made it convincing. Jay unable to look at him properly at his desk. Jay turning red at the sight of a hand on a document.

Heat and proximity, Ian had told himself. Gratitude. Hero worship. The aftershock of a holiday.

The holiday itself was the entry he could not reconcile. He had never intended to go near the line—he had meant only to look at it from a permissible distance. But the opportunities kept presenting themselves, and he took each one, and each one left him a step nearer, until one morning his hand was pressed flat against the line and he discovered it was not a wall. It bent like a string under the finger, farther than he would have believed and without one sound of protest, and he was, after all, good with strings. He played this one masterfully, making sure every push would land well before he made it. Every one landed. Jay practically melted under his fingers, met each near-transgression with joy that beamed until it nearly blinded, and Ian had wanted to make him happy so much that in the end each new push barely needed justifying at all.

He had built explanations for Jay's reactions as if he were a failing company building special-purpose vehicles, one thin structure after another to keep the liability off the balance sheet. It had been almost impressive, as exercises in self-deception went. He could have taught a seminar.

The conclusion had been obvious for some time. Jay had feelings for him, and they were already deep. Ian had met the eyes of men who wanted him. He knew what it looked like.

He had expected, in the rare moments he let himself imagine this, that if he ever found out for certain that Jay felt something in return, the knowledge would feel like victory. It did not. It frightened him.

Jay had never had a relationship that worked. He had never been valued for the right things in the right order. His was not the feeling of an equal. It was not the confident desire of a man who knew how desire worked, who had wanted and been wanted before.

Jay had come to him broken. Everything good in his new life ran through Ian—the job, the money, the apartment, the safety. The feelings that had grown there were twined into all of that. Admiration. Relief of being rescued. The joy of belonging somewhere at last. It was tender and it was new, and Ian did not for a moment believe Jay could tell where one thread ended and the next began.

That was the trap, and it was laid out in three directions, and every one of them led somewhere Ian could not go.

If he addressed it plainly, if he sat Jay down and said the true thing, Jay would panic. Ian could see it: Jay's face going white, the apology forming before there was anything to apologize for. He would make himself small in order to survive the embarrassment. He might quit, not because he wanted distance but because his sense of fault would demand punishment.

If he pulled back instead, put distance between them to keep Jay safe, the damage would possibly be worse. Jay would not confront him. He would not ask what had changed. He would notice every omission and interpret restraint as rejection, and rejection as proof that the warmth had been temporary after all. He would smile politely, work flawlessly, and begin disappearing.

If Ian moved closer by degrees and let the friendship warm slowly into something more...

He stopped there and was honest with himself, since dawn had already stripped him of most of his defenses.

He wanted to move closer.

He wanted the first astonished second of a kiss and the moment after, when Jay realized he was allowed to have this. He wanted to put a hand at Jay's waist without pretending it corrected posture. He wanted the sound Jay might make if all that bright longing were reciprocated.

The danger was what Jay might believe he owed.

That was the part Ian could not excuse. Jay had never been mercenary, never calculating, never anything but absurdly sincere, but sincerity did not erase the ledger between them. The power imbalance did not change because both of them wanted it. It made it easier to miss. Jay's entire professional life ran through Ian's judgment. Ian had given him shelter. If he reached for more from inside that shelter—and the island had shown how easily the reaching could be dressed as care—what would Jay think refusal cost?

And perhaps the ugliest part was that Jay might not think of refusal at all.

He might give in because pleasing Ian was already braided through his happiness. Because he trusted Ian to know what was best. Because Ian had spent two years becoming the person Jay could follow without fear.

Beyond even that lay the part Ian knew too well. A CFO and his assistant. He understood what happens when one of these things goes wrong, because he had lived the far end of it once. If this ever curdled, and things between people curdled all the time, Jay was the one it would ruin. The junior party was always the one the world destroys. Always. Jay was already someone the world had found easy to injure, and Ian had put considerable effort into being the thing standing between Jay and that. He had no intention of becoming it.

There was only one course left. Ian had spent the night trying to find a better one, something clever, some path that allowed him in without risking harm, but the correct course was to hold the line exactly where it stood.

He would be warm. He would be generous. He would go on giving Jay every good thing he could give—attention, tenderness, the dinners and the runs and the long honest talks, all of it. He would let Jay feel cherished but he would never tip it over the edge. He would stay restrained enough that Jay always felt safe, never cornered, never asked for anything he could not freely refuse.

He would answer trust with trustworthiness. He would be a mentor and a friend. A man Jay could love without consequence.

It was not what he wanted. It was simply the least harmful arrangement available.

What he wanted was not new. It had been present since long enough ago that Ian had stopped marking where it started. What was new was knowing it was returned, and finding that this made nothing easier. He had managed desire without evidence. Managing it now, with the proof complete, required the same thing it had always required, only more of it.

By half past seven he was in the lobby of the Northlight Financial Center. He took the lift to the thirty-fourth floor alone. The doors opened onto the quiet of the early office, the cleaners gone and the analysts not yet in.

Jay was already at his desk. He was always early. He had a coffee going and looked at his screen with a frown of concentration that meant he was halfway into some spreadsheet.

He looked up the moment Ian entered, and everything in him changed. The guard he kept against the whole world lowered, and a real and helpless smile appeared. It was the last of the evidence.

"Good morning. You're in early," Jay said.

"Good morning."

Nothing more. Nothing dangerous. A greeting in an office before a day full of meetings.

Jay's smile remained, radiant and entirely unaware of what it had done. He bent over the calendar, already talking about the auditor call and the analyst meeting at four. The gloom that had followed them back from the island seemed, finally, to have loosened its hold.

Ian stood beside the desk and listened, and wanted every impossible thing at once.
