# Chapter 49 — [title placeholder]

## Scene 1

The city at twenty past four was colorless, the buildings below stripped of detail by the cloud
cover that had sat over the city for two weeks without lifting. Ian stood at the kitchen window
with his coffee and looked at it.

He had been awake since half past two.

There was nothing wrong with him that could be named. He had gone to bed at eleven, slept for
three hours, and woken in the dark with the particular alertness of a man who has been avoiding
a calculation and whose mind has finally decided to do the arithmetic without him.

He set down the cup and let himself think.

---

He began with the hot springs.

Jay drunk and warm and clinging: *hold me for the rest of my life.* Ian had filed that under
temporary, under the alcohol and the cold and the particular intimacy of the very exhausted. He
had held Jay through the night and in the morning had given nothing back when Jay apologized,
and he had watched Jay's relief and had noted, even then, that it was not entirely what it
appeared to be. Jay had not been relieved to be told it was nothing. He had been relieved to be
given permission to pretend.

Then the island. Ten days, no titles, nothing between them but the geography and the hours and
the fact that Jay had been happy in a way he wasn't anywhere else. Not the measured happiness
Jay produced at the office. Something deeper and more total — the way he had looked up from the
breakfast terrace the first morning, sea laid out in three colors, and then looked at Ian. That
was not the look of an employee taking stock of a supervisor. Ian had been looked at by
subordinates for fifteen years. It didn't look like that.

Last week: the sauna. Jay watching him in long, helpless passes that Ian had told himself were
heat and proximity, and had nearly convinced himself of, except that heat and proximity
explained a minute. Not ten. Not the particular quality of Jay's attention, which was not
incidental. Ian had met the eyes of men who wanted him before. He knew what it looked like.

And then the hundred smaller items he had been refusing to total: Jay's face each time Ian
walked into a room, which was not the alertness of an employee but something prior to that,
faster. Jay checking his watch before Friday dinners with the patience of someone counting
minutes. Jay saying *our* about their runs and then catching himself. The half-finished
sentence at the gilded restaurant three weeks ago — Jay's mouth stopping before the words that
had been lined up behind it. *I miss being yours.* Ian had watched him close that door and had
not looked past it.

He looked now.

The accounting was done. Jay was not grateful. Not devoted to a mentor in the way that
gratitude could sometimes resemble the other thing. The feelings Ian had been watching accrue
like interest over two years were not admiration or occupational loyalty, and had not been that
for some time. Jay had feelings for him, and they had teeth, and they were already deep.

Ian stood at the kitchen window and took this in. Outside, the city went on being lightless.

He was not triumphant. He had known, somewhere below the line of what he permitted himself to
conclude, and the knowing had not made him happy. It frightened him.

---

He thought about what Jay's feelings were made of.

Jay had never had a relationship that worked. Had never been valued for the right things in the
right order. His previous employer had given him a man in authority who treated attraction as
license, and Jay had fled. Ian had given him the opposite: a desk that belonged to him, work
that was seen rather than his face, a year and then another of being taken seriously by someone
who had, from the beginning, no interest in anything else. Jay trusted him absolutely. In two
years, Ian had never once used that trust wrongly.

The feelings that had grown there were twined into all of that. They were not the confident
desire of someone who knew how desire worked, who had a frame for it, who had wanted and been
wanted without consequence before. They were something more tender than that, and more fragile
— safety that had grown warm, admiration that had grown complicated. A man who had never truly
been safe discovering that he was safe with one particular person, and arriving at a feeling he
probably couldn't have named clearly if asked.

If Ian named it for him, Jay might panic. Might read it as a demand, as the beginning of the
same dynamic he had fled — power that ran in one direction and expected something in return.

If Ian withdrew, put distance where there had been warmth, Jay would read it as rejection. Ian
had watched Jay's recovery from Gal Gray's accusation and knew exactly how far confidence could
fall in a day, and how long the climb back took.

If Ian moved closer — and he wanted to; he was honest enough at twenty past four in the dark to
admit it clearly — the power imbalance did not change because both of them wanted it. It
acquired a different shape. A CFO and his own assistant. Ian knew this story. He had watched it
told in arbitration records, in the depositions that followed when these arrangements went wrong
in the ways they reliably did. The junior party was the one the world made pay. Always. Ian had
survived the Sam Connelly scandal, eventually; he had spent eight years poor and had come back.
Jay's position was not that. Jay was already someone the world had found easy to injure, and
Ian had put considerable effort into being the thing standing between Jay and that. He had no
intention of becoming it.

The wanting was not new. It had been present since long enough ago that Ian had stopped marking
where it started. What was new was knowing it was returned, and finding that this made nothing
easier. He had managed desire without evidence. Managing it now, with the proof complete,
required the same thing it had always required, only more of it.

---

The decision had been obvious before he finished thinking it through. He had known it since the
hot springs, probably, without the honesty to name it clearly. He would stay the course. Be
what Jay needed: close enough that Jay felt it, held back enough that Jay was never frightened
by what the nearness was. Jay was allowed to feel what he felt. Ian was not required to perform
ignorance, but he was also not required to make it into a problem that needed solving. He could
carry this. He was good at carrying things.

The correct decision. He did not doubt it.

---

By quarter past six the cloud cover had gone from black to a dark pewter that promised nothing.
Ian dressed in the near-dark, knotted his tie, took his coat from the rack. The commute was
short and the roads empty at that hour. He drove through streets still wet from the previous
night's rain.

He arrived at the thirty-fourth floor at seven forty. The lights were already on — Amy with the
morning's projections, Luc explaining something to Rin using a pen as a pointer, Ela on a call
with her headset across one ear. The office in ordinary motion.

Jay was at his desk, turned toward his monitor. He hadn't looked up.

Ian crossed the floor. Jay looked up.

His face did what it did.

Ian could not have said precisely what changed in Jay's expression in that moment — it was not
a smile, not yet. It was the face of someone whose attention had been waiting, without knowing
it was waiting, and had found what it was waiting for. The slight, involuntary opening. An
alignment.

The November gloom had gone out of him. The two weeks of it — the post-island mourning, the
useless days, the slow repair through greenhouses and gilded restaurants and a hotel pool where
he'd complained at length about the towels — all of that had been worked through, and what was
left was Jay, rested and bright-eyed in the early Monday morning. The late November dark
outside had not lifted. Jay had.

"Good morning," Ian said.

"Good morning." Jay reached for the calendar printout without being asked. "Velder's moved to
ten — they sent the request Friday afternoon. Amy wants fifteen minutes before the Calder
review, and the Nori documents need your attention. Revised terms sheet."

"I saw it."

"There's coffee."

"Thank you."

Ian went into his office. The calendar on the second monitor was already updated — Velder at
ten, Amy's slot cleared before Calder, the Nori file flagged with a note in Jay's handwriting.
Ian looked at it, and went to the market report.

Through the glass, Jay had returned to his screen. The floor moved around him: calls, low
conversation, a brief exchange that ended in laughter. Ian read the first page of the report
without taking it in, and on the second attempt read it properly, and by the third item he was
working.

The windows gave him November: pewter, low cloud, the city enduring. He had no complaint
with that.
