## Scene 3: A Smaller Tree

The office tree was a desktop thing, a meter of artificial fir in a weighted pot that lived eleven months of the year in the bottom of Ian's cupboard and came out, each December, to be dressed by exactly two people.

It had its own box. Not the team's boxes — a separate one, smaller, that Jay kept now, with a green ribbon tied around it that had started as a joke about wrapping and become the thing he used to find it on the shelf. Inside were the ornaments the floor never saw. A glass fox from the first year, because Ian had said the word *cunning* about a deal and Jay had bought the fox the next day without explaining it. A tiny brass violin from the second. A flat enamel mango that Jay had ordered off a website at one in the morning during the worst of the post-island slump, and had said nothing about, and had simply added to the box, where Ian had found it and said nothing about either, and which now meant the whole of November without a single word attached.

"Rule one," Jay said, lifting the lid. "We don't repeat last year."

"Last year was blue."

"Last year was very blue. We over-corrected. This year we under-correct."

"Toward what."

"Toward whatever's in the box and a general policy of restraint." He looked at the gold grapefruit-sized bauble Ian had carried in from the floor display under his arm, against all protocol, and was now holding with intent. "That is not restraint. That is the opposite of the brief."

"It's the best one. The team won't miss a single bauble out of two hundred."

"You stole an ornament from the staff tree to bring to your own."

"I reallocated a strategic asset." Ian hung it dead center, where it took up a third of the little tree and was patently absurd, and stood back with the satisfaction of a man who had ruined something on purpose. "There. Now we under-correct around that."

So they did. The string of small white lights went on first, because lights went on first, that rule held even here. Then the box gave up its contents one at a time, and the two of them moved around the meter of fir in the lamplight with the office dark and empty beyond the glass, and there was no schism to broker and no newcomer to teach and nobody on the far side of the wall. Just the two of them and a small bright tree and the gold monstrosity at the heart of it, and the slow procession of objects that meant things neither of them said.

Jay hung the fox. Ian hung the violin. Jay turned the mango to face into the room and adjusted it twice and let it be.

It was, Ian thought, watching him do it, the easiest thing in the world and the most dangerous. Jay was a hand's width away, flushed from the day, his hair gone slightly wrong from an afternoon of reaching into boxes, entirely unguarded in the way he only was when a door had shut on the rest of the world. He looked up from the mango with the lamplight on him and a question half-formed and happy on his face, and Ian understood with the flat certainty he now carried everywhere that he could close the distance and Jay would not move away. That Jay would, in fact, come the rest of it. That whatever was in Jay's face right now was not gratitude and not deference and would answer anything Ian offered it, gladly, tonight, here.

And Ian hung an ornament instead, and kept his hands to the tree, and was the steady thing he had decided in the dark to be. The wanting did not lessen for being refused. He had stopped expecting it to. He had only learned, in the week since he'd worked it all out, that the refusal could be its own kind of tenderness if he did it gently enough — that he could stand this close and want this much and give nothing back across it but a warm and ordinary evening, and that the giving of the ordinary evening, the withholding of everything underneath it, was the most careful love he had in him. Jay would never know it was love. That was the design. Jay would only know he was safe, and cherished, and decorating a foolish tree with someone who would never once put a foot wrong, and would go home tonight whole.

"You've gone quiet," Jay said. "You do that. Right when it's going well."

"I'm admiring the work."

"You're admiring your stolen bauble."

"That too."

Jay finished with the last ornament — a paper star he'd made badly and would not let Ian replace, a third rule of the box — and stood back beside him to look at the thing they'd made. The little tree was ridiculous and theirs and the gold heart of it threw the lamplight around the dark office like something far grander than a meter of cupboard fir had any right to.

There was a quiet then, the good kind, and Jay let it run a little long before he broke it, and when he broke it his words came out faster than usual and slightly out of order, the way they did when he'd decided something in advance and was making himself say it before the nerve went.

"I wanted to ask you something. About Christmas. I've been meaning to, I keep — anyway. Come to mine. For Christmas. The actual day. I know it's small, the flat, it's not — it's nothing like yours, and I'm not a tenth the cook you are, but I want to do it. Properly. Have you over. If you don't already have — I mean, if you're free. You're probably not free. Forget the last bit, just. Come to mine for Christmas."

He'd looked at the tree for most of it and at Ian for the end of it, and there was the nerve in his face and under the nerve a determination that hadn't been there last December, a man who'd decided he was allowed to want this and was going to ask for it out loud even if it cost him.

Ian said yes before the sentence had finished cooling.

"Yes. I'd like that. Thank you."

It came out level, almost offhand, and he heard how level it was and could not make it otherwise, because the truth underneath it would not have fit in the room. He had spent the better part of the week on this exact question, against his own better judgment — lying awake doing the one piece of arithmetic he had no business doing, running the version where Jay didn't ask, where the day came and Ian spent it as he'd spent every recent one, well-provisioned and alone in a large quiet apartment, declining Leo's invitation out of a preference he could no longer name, and the version had been bleak enough that he'd made himself stop. He'd told himself not to count on it. And then he'd counted on it anyway, because he knew Jay, knew the way December reliably turned the heat up under everything Jay felt, knew that Jay had been less careful with himself all month, brighter, bolder, asking for things. He had been almost sure the question was coming. Being right about it steadied nothing. The relief had simply arrived under the calm and stayed there, vast and silent, where Jay couldn't see it.

"Yeah?" Jay said. "Just like that?"

"Just like that. Did you expect a negotiation?"

"A bit. You usually have conditions. No party hats. No speeches."

"For your flat I waive the conditions. Reduced quorum. I'll come and I'll behave and I'll bring the wine, and that's my only term, you don't argue about the wine."

"I wasn't going to argue about the wine." Jay was grinning now, the nerve gone, replaced by a pleasure so plain it was almost difficult to look at directly. "I can't believe you said yes that fast. I had a whole second half. I had reasons prepared."

"Keep them. You'll need them for something."

"I really thought you'd have plans."

"My plans," Ian said, "were to be talked into yours. You've saved us both a great deal of time."

They turned off the office lights and left the small tree glowing in the dark for nobody, because it was theirs and it could glow for nobody if they liked, and they took the lift down through the quiet building into the cold. Jay talked the whole way about menus he wasn't qualified to attempt and a flat too small for a proper tree and whether Ian liked goose or found it pretentious, and Ian answered every question and held, behind the answers, the single fixed fact of where he would be on the twenty-fifth, in a small warm flat two streets from his own, watching the person he wanted be happy across a table he wasn't allowed to reach over, and being glad of it, genuinely and completely glad, which was the part that would have frightened him if he'd let it.
