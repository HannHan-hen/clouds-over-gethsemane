## Scene 4: Costume Drama

The pretext was archived client correspondence.

It was real, which was what made it a good pretext. There genuinely existed, in a folder on a server, a set of pre-2015 discretionary notes that the auditors might one day want cross-referenced against the old engagement letters, and it was genuinely the sort of tedious reconciling that Jay was genuinely good at, and it genuinely did not require a Saturday, or Jay's coat on Ian's hook, or two pots of coffee, or any of the rest of it. Both of them knew this. Neither of them said it. Jay arrived at eleven with his work bag over his shoulder and the bag stayed zipped by the door, and that was the last anyone thought of the archived correspondence for the rest of the day.

They got perhaps twenty minutes into it, out of decency, sitting at Ian's table with one laptop open between them, before the subject of Christmas reabsorbed the entire morning.

"I've been thinking about the food," Jay said, which meant he had been thinking about nothing else.

"Have you."

"I'm doing it. All of it. That's the arrangement, that's why it's at mine. You're not allowed to show up with half a catered dinner and a better idea. I know you. You'll try to improve it on the day."

"I would never improve your dinner."

"You improve everything. You can't help it. You'd taste the gravy and go quiet and then there'd be a shallot situation." Jay closed the laptop, which ended the pretense formally. "I want to cook for you. Badly, probably. The point isn't that it'll be as good as yours, it won't be, I've eaten yours, I have no illusions. The point is I want to be the one who does it. For once I want to have you over instead of being had over. Does that make sense."

It made a great deal of sense, and it landed somewhere in Ian he kept the lights off in. He was a man who provided. He arranged, supplied, anticipated, solved — flats and soup and thermometers and hotel suites and a whole printed album of a holiday so that Jay would have something to hold. He gave, and Jay received, and that traffic ran one direction by Ian's quiet design, and here was Jay asking, plainly, to send something back the other way. To host. To feed him. To be, for one day, the one with something to offer.

Ian could have made it easy and didn't examine why he wanted to.

"It makes sense," he said. "It's your table. I'll come empty-handed and hungry and I'll praise the gravy whatever the gravy does."

"You'll mean it, though. Don't praise it like a client."

"I'll mean it. I'm bringing wine, that's non-negotiable, but I'll arrive with nothing else and no opinions, and you'll cook, and I'll be a guest in your home, and I'll do whatever guests do. What do guests do? I'm out of practice."

"They sit where they're told and they don't reorganize the kitchen."

"That sounds restful. I'll try it."

The reconciling did not resume. Lunch happened instead, assembled out of Ian's fridge in defiance of the work neither of them was doing, and then the afternoon went where Jay's afternoons went when no one stopped them, which was to the television and the long-running costume drama he was three seasons deep in and unashamed of.

Ian had given up pretending he didn't watch it too. He had opinions about the second son's marriage prospects. He kept them to himself with difficulty.

It was halfway through an episode that the masquerade came on — a great painted ballroom, candles in their hundreds, the whole cast in feathered half-masks misbehaving behind them, a countess doing something scandalous to a viscount under cover of not being recognizable, which fooled no one, which was the entire point.

"It's a shame, really," Jay said, his feet up, entirely content. "That people don't do these anymore. Masquerades. The masks, the whole — all of it. It looks wonderful. Nobody does it now."

"They do, actually."

Jay turned his head. "They don't."

"They do. They're awful." Ian said it with the certainty of a man reporting from inside the awfulness. "They've never stopped. There's a circuit of them, two or three a winter, dressed up as charity or as somebody's anniversary, and they are uniformly excruciating. Expensive past any sense, affected beyond belief, and packed wall to wall with people who have bought taste recently and want it witnessed."

This did not put Jay off in the slightest. It did the opposite. He muted the countess mid-scandal and turned the whole of his attention on Ian, which Ian had long ago learned was the most dangerous thing in any room.

"No. Tell me. What are they actually like. Don't soften it, I want the real one."

So Ian told him, because Jay had asked, and because there was nothing Ian could deny him that cost this little.

"Start with the masks. The idea is mystery. The reality is four hundred people who all want to be looked at wearing objects specifically designed to make them more conspicuous. Feathers a foot high. Crystals. There's always one man in a full carnival mask with a beak who thinks he's the most interesting person present and is, in fact, the most tiresome, and you will be cornered by him, it's a law. The women's are better and worse — genuinely beautiful, some of them, and worn by people who spent more on the mask than I spent on my first car and want you to know the figure."

"Go on."

"The costumes. Nobody's told whether it's a costume ball or merely black tie with masks, so it's both, and the room is half men in ordinary dinner jackets and half a man dressed as a doge. There's always a doge. Overdesigned to the last thread — trains, brocade, a woman who can't sit down for the architecture of what she's wearing and so stands all night by a pillar being admired and resenting it."

Jay had pulled a cushion into his lap and folded over it, rapt. "The dancing. Tell me they dance."

"They attempt to. Someone insists on the proper court dances because it's that sort of evening, and almost no one knows them, so you get a floor of very wealthy people walking the wrong way into each other with great confidence. There's a hired few who can actually do it, brought in to make it look intentional. And a champagne tower — there's always a champagne tower, a pyramid of glasses they pour from the top so it cascades, and it photographs beautifully and tastes of warm nothing by the time it reaches you, and someone, every single time, has had too much and threatens the tower's structural integrity around midnight."

"It sounds amazing."

"It sounds amazing. It is interminable. The secret of those evenings, Jay, the thing under all the feathers, is that nearly everyone there is bored to the back teeth and performing the opposite at enormous expense. Half the room is watching the other half to see whether anyone's enjoying it more than they are. The masks don't hide anything. They just give all that watching somewhere to hide behind."

"You've been to a lot of them."

"I've escaped a lot of them. There's a difference. I learned where the side doors were very young." Ian glanced at him, at the open delight on his face, the cushion clutched to him, a man who had grown up where the fanciest coffee came from a gas station listening to a report from a world of doges and champagne towers as if it were a fairy story, which, told properly, it nearly was. "You'd hate it," Ian said, and did not believe it, and watched Jay not believe it either.

"I would not. I'd love every awful second of it. The beak man. The doge. I'd find the side door eventually but I'd take the long way round first."

"You'd be the only person in the building actually enjoying himself. They'd hate you for it. You'd be the most conspicuous thing there and you wouldn't even need a mask."

He hadn't meant it to come out quite so plainly. Jay went a little pink and looked back at the screen and unmuted the countess, who was still mid-scandal, and the conversation folded itself back into the episode, but Jay's foot tapped against the cushion for a while after, and Ian could see him filing the whole picture away somewhere bright, the masks and the dances and the tower, to be taken back out later and turned over in better light. He logged it too, more carefully than he let on — that Jay wanted this, the costume and the candlelight and the long way round before the side door. He filed it under things he might one day be able to give. The list of those was shorter than it used to be. He was learning to be glad of every item that survived the cut.
