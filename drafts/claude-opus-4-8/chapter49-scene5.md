## Scene 5: The Company Gift

The office party was a Thursday, which told you everything about the office party.

A gala got a Saturday and five hundred people and fireworks. The internal year-end thing got a Thursday evening, the big meeting room with the partition folded back, a caterer who did the same canapés for every firm in the building, and a team that had spent the whole week closing the year and had, collectively, about ninety minutes of celebration left in them before the trains started looking attractive. Nobody minded. By December everyone was tired in the specific way the work made you tired, and a party you could leave by nine was not a disappointment, it was a mercy.

They came anyway, all of them, because it was the one the whole floor went to. Max held court by the canapés and pronounced the tiny tarts a war crime and ate eleven of them. Ela and Kim arrived eight minutes apart in a fooling-no-one fashion that the floor had stopped pretending to fall for years ago. Tia discovered that the bar, unlike the gala's, was free and unsupervised, and made it her personal mission. Rin tracked Ray to the party — Ray had come, under what threat from Leo no one knew — and stood near him radiating the quiet triumph of a man who had befriended a cat. Amy left at half eight to relieve the babysitter and told four separate people that Liv's youngest was finally over the fever, which was the most genuinely good news anyone delivered all night.

Jay worked the room the way he worked the tree, moving between knots of people, refilling the shy ones' confidence, keeping Max away from the speeches. Ian stood at the edge of it with a glass he wasn't drinking and watched the year wind itself down and answered, twice, the polite question of whether he had plans for the holiday with a polite nothing, and held the real answer where it was warm.

The company gift came out near the end, the way it did every year. The firm gave one — Leo's tradition, Leo's flourish, an envelope per head handed round with the last of the wine, and the floor had a running and entirely unscientific history of trying to guess it in advance. Two years ago it had been a weekend somewhere. Another year, a hamper so large Jay had eaten out of it into February. Last year an experience voucher, the spa-and-treatment kind, a day at a place with robes and a pool, redeemable any time in the new year.

That last one was the one Jay had his hopes on, and he was not subtle with himself about why.

He'd done the math the moment the envelopes appeared. An experience voucher was a thing you used, and a thing you used was a thing you could use with someone, and the sauna voucher from Rus had taught him exactly how that worked — how a gift could be a door, how *it would be a waste of the money otherwise* was a complete and bulletproof reason to spend an evening somewhere warm and quiet with Ian and call it administration. The whole of November had turned on a voucher like that. If the firm handed him a spa day he would have, ready-made, a clean and unimpeachable excuse to take Ian somewhere again, with the cost already justified and nobody's eyebrow raised, least of all his own.

He opened the envelope. It was a cash card.

A perfectly good cash card, a generous one, loaded with a sum that was nothing to the firm and a real and welcome thing to him. Tia, two seats over, made a noise of pure approval and immediately began allocating hers out loud. It was, by every sensible measure, the better gift. You could put it against anything. It was money, and money was useful, and a man who had spent years paying down loans a hundred at a time knew precisely how useful.

Jay was disappointed for about ten seconds.

The disappointment had nothing to do with the money and everything to do with the door that hadn't opened. No voucher. No built-in reason. No clean excuse waiting in an envelope to do the asking for him.

And then, in the eleventh second, somewhere under the tired noise of the winding-down party, he decided something, and the deciding was so simple it surprised him.

He'd buy it himself.

The spa day, the warm quiet place with the robes and the pool — it existed whether or not the firm handed it to him. He had a cash card in his hand and money behind it and there was nothing, nothing at all, stopping him from booking the thing and saying, *I got us this, come on, it'd be a shame to go alone.* He didn't need the firm to manufacture his excuse. He could just want it, and arrange it, and offer it.

He knew exactly what it was. He turned it over in his head with his eyes open, the way he hadn't let himself look at anything for two years. A spa, the two of them, robes, a quiet pool, a whole afternoon with no work between them and no holiday rules to make it not count. That was not a thing colleagues bought each other. That was not a mentor-and-assistant outing, and it was not, if anyone saw the booking, a thing that would read as anything other than what couples did, two names on one reservation at a place built for exactly that. He knew how it looked. He knew how it would feel, sitting beside him in the heat with no document to pretend at.

He was going to buy it anyway.

Some governor in him that had run for two years on gratitude and fear and the certainty that wanting was the one thing he was never allowed had finally, sometime around the island, burned out, and it had not come back on, and he was beginning to suspect it wasn't going to. He still didn't think it could go anywhere. A man like Ian, and a man like him — the old arithmetic still gave the old answer, that he was not the sort of person who got to have this, that whatever he felt was his to carry and not to act on. But the not-acting had quietly changed shape. He could carry the feeling and still buy the voucher. He could know it was hopeless and still want the afternoon. The hopelessness no longer reached all the way down to his hands, and his hands wanted to do this, and so he was going to let them.

Across the room Ian caught his look and raised an eyebrow at the cash card, a small dry question — *good?* — and Jay grinned and raised the envelope in answer, *good*, and gave nothing else away. He'd surprise him with it. After the new year, when the bookings opened. He'd find the right one, the quiet one, and he'd buy it, and he'd hand it over with some flimsy line about waste and money and the cost being a shame, the exact flimsy line Ian himself used, and he'd watch Ian fail to find a reason to say no.

The party broke up before nine, as predicted and as hoped. Coats, scarves, the lift down in shifts, everyone scattering toward trains with the year nearly behind them and a long warm holiday in front. Jay walked the two streets home through the cold with the cash card in his pocket and the whole bright machinery of it turning over in him — the spa, the day, the offer he was going to make — and for once the cold didn't touch him at all, and he didn't try to talk himself out of a single part of it.
