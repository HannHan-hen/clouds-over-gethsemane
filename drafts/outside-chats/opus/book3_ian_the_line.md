The clock read 04:50. Ian had been awake since three, and before that he had slept in fragments that did not deserve the name.

He sat in the dark of his penthouse with a glass of water he had not touched. The city spread out below the windows, a field of small lights running down toward the river. At this hour Maincross belonged to no one. The towers of the financial district stood unlit except for the red beacons at their summits, and the streets far below were empty.

He had come out here to think, and now he could no longer avoid the thing he had been thinking around for weeks.

He made himself do it properly. He was good at this. He spent his days assembling scattered facts into a single clear picture, and he had refused to apply that skill to the one subject that mattered most. So he applied it now.

He started with the way Jay looked at him. It was not the open admiration of the early months, when Jay had watched him the way a junior watches a senior he wants to impress. Something had changed. Jay looked at him now and then looked away, and color came into his face when he was caught, and he found small tasks to occupy himself. A man did not blush at his mentor.

Then the matter of being alone together. Jay was a quiet man in a crowd, careful, watchful, always half braced for someone to turn on him. But alone with Ian he changed. He talked more. He laughed at things. He let his guard down in a way Ian had seen him grant to almost no one. On the island Jay had been happiest in the hours when the rest of the world fell away, and he had not hidden it.

Then the touching. Jay moved toward him. On the cruise, in the sauna, in a hundred small moments, when Ian put a hand to his shoulder or steered him through a crowd, Jay did not pull away the way a wary man would. He tilted toward it. He stayed.

Then the words. The ones Ian had tried hardest to file under drunkenness and forget. Jay outside the dining hall at the hot springs, holding onto him and crying, asking to be held for the rest of his life. Ian had told him the next morning that it was nothing, a harmless thing the liquor had said. He had told himself the same. It was a lie in both directions.

And under all of it, the trust. That was the part he could not argue with. Jay handed him things no one else was allowed to touch—his fear, his shame, the worst hours of his life. He let Ian see him sick and frightened and undone. A man did not do that for someone he merely respected.

Ian set the glass down on the table.

The conclusion had been obvious for some time. He had simply refused to write it down. Jay was in love with him. Or something near enough to it that the distinction did not help.

He had expected, in the rare moments he let himself imagine this, that the knowledge would feel like victory. It did not. It frightened him.

Because Jay's feeling was not the feeling of an equal, and Ian understood that with the cold clarity he brought to everything. Jay had come to him broken by another man, by an office that had used him and thrown him away. Everything good in Jay's new life ran through Ian—the job, the money, the apartment two streets over, the safety. Whatever Jay felt was braided together with all of it. Gratitude. Admiration. The simple animal relief of being rescued. The hunger of a man who had never belonged anywhere to belong somewhere at last. It was tender and it was new, and Ian did not for a moment believe Jay could tell where one thread ended and the next began.

That was the trap, and it was laid out in three directions, and every one of them led somewhere Ian could not go.

If he named it plainly, if he sat Jay down and said the true thing, Jay would panic. He would hear an accusation in it, or a demand, or proof that he had ruined the one safe place he had. He might run. Men like Jay ran.

If he pulled back instead, put distance between them to keep Jay safe from his own confusion, Jay would read it as rejection. He would decide he had done something wrong, the way he decided that about everything, and the brightness Ian had spent two years coaxing out of him would go dark again.

And if he did the third thing, the thing some traitor part of him wanted, if he moved closer by degrees and let the friendship warm into something with a different name, then Jay, who could refuse him nothing, who already believed he owed Ian the world, would think he was meant to give more than he truly wanted to give. He would offer his body the way he offered everything else, out of devotion and obligation and the terror of disappointing the man who held his whole life in trust. And Ian would never, as long as he lived, be able to tell whether any of it was freely chosen.

That he wanted Jay did not help. It made everything worse.

It would have been easier if Ian felt nothing, if he could be only the steady older man doing the decent thing. But he wanted Jay with an intensity that disgusted him for its timing. He thought about him constantly. He had played the violin for him and cooked for him and built a whole architecture of small rituals around him, the Friday dinners and the Tuesday runs, and he had called it friendship and known that it was not.

Both of them wanting each other should have been the simple part. In another life it would have been everything. But wanting did not erase the ledger between them. He was still the man who signed off on Jay's raises. He was still the reason Jay could sleep at night. The power did not vanish because Ian's heart was in it too. If anything it grew teeth. A subordinate could refuse a cold superior. How was Jay supposed to refuse a warm one he adored?

He had done this once. Not this, exactly, but near enough. He had let himself love a man who worked beneath him, and he had told himself it was equal, and it had ended in a scandal that cost him almost everything and cost the other man more. He had sworn never again. He had built his whole adult life on that lesson. And here he was at five in the morning, in love with his assistant, watching the sky over Maincross go from black to the first dirty grey.

So. If he could not name it, and could not flee it, and could not have it, then there was only one course left, and Ian was a man who took the only course the moment he saw it.

He would hold the line exactly where it stood.

He would be warm. He would be generous. He would go on giving Jay every good thing he could give—attention, care, shelter, the dinners and the runs and the long honest talks, all of it. He would let Jay feel cherished, because Jay deserved to feel cherished and had spent thirty-one years starved of it. But he would never tip it over the edge. He would stay restrained enough that Jay always felt safe, never cornered, never asked for anything he could not freely refuse.

He would be the mentor, the friend, the shelter. He would be a thing Jay could love without consequence, the way one loves a teacher, or an older brother, a fixed point that asks nothing back. Jay could pour all of it into him and lose nothing. Ian would carry the rest alone. He had carried worse.

It was not what he wanted. It was the only version of caring for Jay that did not risk harming him, and Ian knew, sitting there as the room turned grey, that this was the line he would not cross even for himself. Especially for himself.

He stood. The decision had the cold comfort of all his decisions, clean and final and joyless. He went to shower and dress for work.

By half past seven he was in the lobby of the Northlight Financial Center, his suit pressed and his hair in order, every outward sign of a man who had slept eight hours. He took the lift to the thirty-fourth floor alone. The doors opened onto the quiet of the early office, the cleaners gone and the analysts not yet in.

Jay was already at his desk.

Of course he was. Jay was always early. He had a coffee going and his screen open and a small frown of concentration that meant he was halfway into some spreadsheet. He had not heard the lift.

Then he looked up.

And Ian watched it happen, the thing he had spent all night cataloguing, played out in a single second in front of him. Jay saw him, and everything in him changed. The careful guard he kept against the whole world broke open into something unguarded and glad, a real and helpless brightness, the look of a man whose day had only now begun in earnest.

"Morning. You're in early. There's coffee, if you want it. I made the good kind," Jay said.

"Good morning. Thank you. I'd like that," Ian said.

And there it was. Every doubt he might have carried out of the night, every comforting maybe, gone in the space of a smile. He had been right. Jay was in love with him.

It confirmed everything. It wounded him exactly as much.

He went to get his coffee so that he would not have to keep looking at him.
