# Clouds Over Gethsemane — Book Three

---

The city below the penthouse was still dark. Ian stood at the floor-to-ceiling window with a glass of water he had not drunk and watched the first grey suggestion of light press against the eastern edge of the skyline. He had been awake for most of the night.

He had not been ill. He had not been anxious about work. He had simply lain in bed, stared at the ceiling, and eventually given up on the idea of sleep entirely.

He put the glass down on the low table beside the window and crossed his arms.

The problem was that he had been very careful, for a very long time, not to look directly at certain things. He was good at that. He had built a career partly on the ability to assess a situation clearly and then set aside whatever in it was inconvenient. You did not last long in rooms like the ones he inhabited by letting sentiment cloud your reading of events. And so he had taken everything that Jay showed him and filed it neatly under categories he could live with: gratitude, professional loyalty, admiration, the ordinary warmth of a person who had been treated decently for the first time in years.

He had believed it. Or he had made himself believe it.

He walked to the kitchen, set the glass in the sink, and stood with his hands on the edge of the counter.

The staring. He had noticed it long ago and decided not to notice it again. The way Jay would be in the middle of something—writing, reading, answering a question from someone on the team—and then Ian would say his name, and Jay would look up, and for a moment before the professional expression assembled itself there would be something else entirely. Something quick and bright and unguarded. He had always attributed it to focus being broken. But it happened when Ian entered a room Jay was already in, before Ian had spoken at all.

The blushes. Jay blushed easily and about many things. That had always been true. But Ian had learned, without meaning to, the difference between the blush that came from embarrassment and the one that came from being watched. He knew which one appeared when Ian paid him a particular kind of attention.

The happiness. That was the one he least wanted to examine. Jay was happier when they were alone together than at any other time. Ian had watched it happen often enough to be certain of it—the slight dropping away of tension, the ease that came into the way he sat and spoke, the way he laughed more freely. Jay had friends now, people at the office he liked and trusted, and he was different with them than he had been when he arrived. But that ease, the specific ease of being alone with Ian, was its own category.

The island.

Ian pressed his fingertips harder against the counter's edge.

He did not want to think about the island. He had been thinking about the island, or rather about certain hours of the island, for weeks in a way that was not productive and that he had been managing to suppress during working hours. But at four in the morning with no sleep and nothing else to occupy him, suppression became more difficult.

Jay had held onto his arm in the water and laughed. Jay had sat beside him every night at dinner and looked at him across the candlelight and talked about his grandfather and his family and the things he had wanted before he learned to stop wanting them, and Ian had sat there and let himself believe he was simply a good listener, that this was what Jay did with everyone he trusted. But he had watched Jay with Luc, with Max, with Rus. He talked easily with all of them. What he gave Ian was not that.

And then the night he had drunk too much.

Ian moved away from the counter and sat down on the low sofa facing the window. The light was still grey but it was strengthening.

Jay had cried. Jay had clung to his arm with his face turned into Ian's shoulder and said, in the voice of someone admitting to something they had been holding for a very long time, *hold me for the rest of your life*. And Ian had told him, gently, carefully, that he had him. And Jay had gone to sleep against him.

Ian had sat very still for a long time after Jay fell asleep, afraid to move in case he woke him. That was what he had told himself. He had also told himself it was nothing, that people said things when they were drunk, that Jay would wake up mortified and the kindest thing was to treat it as harmless so that Jay did not feel he had damaged something.

He had believed that too. Or he had tried to.

Jay had a crush on him.

Ian said it to himself plainly, the way he would state a figure in a report, and waited to see what he felt.

What he felt was not triumphant. He had imagined, during the years when he had thought about Jay more than was appropriate and then disciplined himself not to, that if he ever found out for certain that Jay felt something in return, he would feel something like relief, or vindication, or simple joy. He felt none of those things. He felt frightened.

Because Jay's feelings were not the feelings of someone who had been in love before and knew how to handle it. They were not the confident, practiced desire of an equal. Ian had spent enough time with Jay to know what he was looking at. Jay had come to him having been frightened and used and left out of things he had wanted. He had come to Ian looking for safety, found it, and then apparently found something else as well, something that had grown slowly and without Jay particularly intending it, braided together with all the rest—the gratitude and the safety and the belonging—until Jay probably could not tell where one thing ended and another began. His feelings for Ian were tender and inexperienced and bound up with Ian's role in his life in a way that was not simple to separate out.

That was precisely what made it dangerous.

If Ian told him he knew—the wrong way, too directly, making Jay feel that he had revealed himself and been caught—Jay would panic. Ian had watched Jay panic before. He would withdraw behind perfect professional behavior and it would be months before they found their way back.

If Ian pulled back to protect them both from an unequal situation, Jay would think he had done something wrong. Jay's first assumption in any difficulty was always that he had failed someone. Ian could not give him that.

And if Ian moved closer, slowly, deliberately, which was what he wanted to do—what he had wanted to do for longer than he intended to admit—he would have to be very careful about what he allowed Jay to think. Ian's position, Ian's money, Ian's history of protecting him, all of it pressed on the same bruise that Jay had carried since before they met, the bruise that made him feel he owed things, that security had a price, that kindness came with a shape that had to be fit into. He would have to be certain, completely certain, that Jay understood he was not being asked to offer more than he genuinely wanted to give. That certainty was not something Ian could establish quickly or easily.

He wanted Jay. That was simple enough. He had been clear-eyed about that for long enough to have stopped finding it a surprise. But the wanting did not change the architecture of the problem. Both of them wanting each other did not make the power difference disappear. Ian was still the man who had hired him, promoted him, arranged his apartment and his salary and the shape of his daily life. Jay was still the person who had arrived on his floor grateful for a desk and a manager who treated him like a person. Those things did not vanish because they also, separately, wanted each other.

So what Ian could do was what he was already doing. He could stay steady. He could be warm without being so warm that Jay felt he was being beckoned toward something he had not chosen consciously. He could make Jay feel safe to have these feelings without ever putting Jay in a position where he felt he needed to act on them or declare them or do anything with them at all. Jay could love him quietly, from where he stood, and Ian could love him back from exactly the same distance, and Jay would never feel the weight of an expectation he did not know how to meet.

Ian was a patient man. He was a man who had learned, through significant effort and significant pain, to want things without demanding them. He could do this. He could be the mentor and the friend and the shelter that Jay could love without consequence, safely and without it ever becoming complicated or frightening or something that cost Jay any piece of the life he had built.

He looked at the city. The light was gold now at the edges. The sky was turning.

He got up, went to the bathroom, and ran a shower cold enough to cut through two hours of sleep, and when he came out the apartment was full of pale morning.

---

He arrived at Turner & Associates at five past eight. The lift opened onto the thirty-fourth floor and the office was already partly populated—Amy at her desk, Kim with headphones on, Ray talking to someone on the phone with the door to the small conference room ajar.

Jay was at his own desk near the window. He was reading something on his screen with one hand curled loosely around his coffee cup. His hair was slightly damp, which meant he had walked part of the way and gotten caught in the morning mist. He was wearing the grey jacket Ian recognized from early spring.

He looked up when the lift doors opened, the way he always did, because he always registered when the lift arrived. And then he saw it was Ian, and something happened to his face that lasted only a second before the professional warmth assembled itself on top.

The second was enough.

Ian walked across the office, set his bag on his desk, and turned to Jay with the ordinary, unhurried composure of a man who had slept well.

"Morning," Ian said. "How was the commute?"

"Damp," Jay said. "The forecast said clear."

"The forecast was wrong."

"As it often is." Jay pulled up the briefing notes and handed them across. "The Kettlebrook review moved to Thursday. Liv flagged it last night."

"Fine." Ian took the notes and read the top line. "Have you eaten?"

"I had something at home."

"That's not a yes." Ian set the notes on his desk. "Order something. We're going to be in meetings until noon."

Jay looked at him for a moment—the look that lasted slightly longer than professional attention required—and then said, "Yes, alright," in the quiet tone that meant he was pleased.

Ian sat down and opened his screen and did not look up again.

It confirmed everything, and it hurt in exactly the way he had expected it to hurt, and he sat with it calmly, because he had made a decision in the grey hours before dawn and he was not a man who unmade his decisions easily.

He would be steady. He would be kind. He would stay where he was.

It was, of the available options, the least dangerous thing he could offer Jay. And so it was what he would do.
