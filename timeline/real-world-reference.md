# Real-World Reference — Weather & Holidays

A real-world almanac to **match against**, not to obey. Weather is a running
theme in this series (wet winters especially), so where it's easy we line the
story's seasons up with what Germany actually had that year. Holidays are here
so the big ones land on the right real dates. Everything in this file is a
**template to lean on**, not canon that overrides the prose — when a scene wants
weather reality didn't supply, the alternative timeline wins.

## Setting

The city is **Maincross** *(work name)* — a renamed **Frankfurt am Main** in an
alternative-reality **European Federation**. So the real-world anchor for all
weather and seasonal feel is **Frankfurt / Hesse / the Rhine-Main region**:
mild, oceanic-leaning climate, grey wet winters with only intermittent snow,
warm changeable summers, the Main valley running through it. "Maincross" reads
as a literal gloss of *Frankfurt* — "the ford/crossing of the Main" — which is
why it fits.

> **The universe is on our side — lean in hard.** When we mapped events to real
> dates, the worry was that real weather would contradict us. It does the
> opposite. The **2016 start year holds**, and the two most weather-dependent
> beats of Book 1 are corroborated by the actual record:
> - **Book 1's end-of-summer heatwave** (Ch18–20, "forty degrees outside,"
>   Jay's studio AC can't cope) sits in **June 2017** — which was one of the
>   most extreme early-summer heatwaves on record across Western Europe.
>   France hit 43.8 °C on 24 June; Germany ran into the upper 30s °C. The real
>   peak (**23–24 June**) lands right on **Ema's graduation (Fri Jun 23, 2017)**.
> - A **second** notorious heatwave ("Lucifer") followed in **August 2017**, so
>   the season stayed hot straight through Book 1's close (early Aug).
>
> So we don't hedge the heat — we play it as a real, record-breaking summer.

## Weather almanac (Frankfurt / Germany, by season)

Seasonal feel only — enough to keep a scene honest. Verify a specific day
before hanging a plot point on it.

### 2016 *(Book 1 opens — Mon Aug 1, 2016)*
- **Winter 2015/16:** mild, wet, grey; little lasting snow. Our default
  Frankfurt winter — matches the "wet winters" instinct.
- **Spring:** turned violently wet at the end. **Late May 2016** brought
  historic flash flooding to southern/western Germany (Braunsbach, Simbach,
  27–29 May) from slow, training thunderstorms — unusually a *spring/early-summer*
  flood rather than a winter one.
- **Summer:** only slightly warmer than average, unsettled; the heavy
  early-summer rain was the story, not heat. Book 1's first months (Aug–Sep)
  sit here.
- **Early autumn:** warm September — Frankfurt touched **32.8 °C in Sep 2016**.
  A late burst of summer is fair game for the early chapters.

### 2017 *(Book 1 ch ~11 → end; Book 2 opens Aug 4)*
- **Winter 2016/17:** cold snap in **January 2017** (genuine European cold
  spell, ice) inside an otherwise mild, damp winter — fits the Jan gala / early
  chapters.
- **Spring:** mild and warm overall, but a sharp **late-April frost** raked
  Germany (vineyards/orchards hit) — a cold bite around the Easter window
  (Easter = 16 Apr) is realistic texture.
- **Early summer:** **record June heatwave** (see lean-in note above) — upper
  30s °C in Germany around 22–24 June.
- **Late summer:** the **August "Lucifer"** heatwave kept things hot through
  Book 1's close and into Book 2's opening days.
- **Autumn:** stormy — windstorms **Xavier (5 Oct)** and **Herwart (29 Oct)**
  swept northern/central Germany. Useful for any blustery autumn scene.

### 2018 *(Book 2 ch ~31 → end; Book 3, Aug–Nov)*
- **Winter/early spring:** dry, and a notably **cold late-Feb / early-March**
  spell ("Beast from the East") — cold and dry rather than wet.
- **Spring–summer:** the big one. **April–July 2018 was the warmest on record**
  in Germany, opening a drought that ran **February to November** and affected
  ~90% of the country. **2018 became the hottest year on German record**
  (annual mean ~10.5 °C) with **record sunshine**.
- **Book 3 implication:** the whole of Book 3 (Aug–end Nov 2018) sits inside a
  **record hot, dry, sunny stretch that refused to break**. The fortieth-birthday
  gala (Sep 22), the late-October hot-springs weekend, and the November island
  trip all play against an unusually long, warm, rainless autumn — lean into
  the drought-summer-that-wouldn't-end mood.

## Holiday template (big ones only)

National/bank holidays are irrelevant to the alternative timeline; we track only
the two that anchor scenes. Dates are the real Western (Gregorian) dates.

### Easter
| Year | Good Friday | Easter Sunday | Easter Monday |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2016 | Fri 25 Mar | Sun 27 Mar | Mon 28 Mar |
| 2017 | Fri 14 Apr | Sun 16 Apr | Mon 17 Apr |
| 2018 | Fri 30 Mar | Sun 1 Apr  | Mon 2 Apr  |
| 2019 | Fri 19 Apr | Sun 21 Apr | Mon 22 Apr |

*(Ch16–17's "Easter weekend, Jay studies alone" = Apr 14–17, 2017 — already
matches.)*

### Christmas
Fixed every year: **24 Dec (Heiligabend) · 25 Dec · 26 Dec**. In the German
frame, **Christmas Eve (the 24th) is the focal evening** — the family meal and
gift exchange — with the 25th and 26th as the quiet holiday days after. Advent /
Christmas-market season runs roughly **late Nov → 23 Dec** (Frankfurt's
Römerberg market is the regional fixture) and is good winter-atmosphere texture.
*(Book 2's Christmas falls Mon 25 Dec, 2017.)*
