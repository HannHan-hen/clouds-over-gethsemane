# Book 1 — Craft Notes: Intent, Execution, and Revision Targets

A working record of *what Book 1 is trying to do*, how well it currently does it, and
the specific places to tighten. Mostly concerned with Ian's arc and the book's themes;
Jay's thread is noted at the end but reserved for a later pass.

This is a craft/intent companion, not a continuity log. For dating issues see
`timeline/book1-timeline.md` (its "drift & flags" section).

---

## Foundation: narration mode and the tightrope

**Narration.** Omniscient, but anchored mostly to Jay. Ian's interior world is *not*
hidden from the reader — we see his struggle even when he shows nothing outwardly. This
is deliberate and load-bearing: the reader is in on a battle that Jay cannot see.

**Why Jay can't see it.** Ian projects coldness *on purpose*, and has for years; the
office takes it for granted and comments on it from Chapter 1. Jay has no baseline for
"normal Ian," so he reads the coldness as ordinary rigid professionalism. Only with time
does Jay notice that Ian is *colder to him specifically* — that he gets the cold shoulder
more often than others — and it begins to bother him. (That perception arc is Jay's, and
is reserved for the later Jay pass.)

**The tightrope.** State Ian's character and the battle underneath it *clearly*, but never
drive the point forcefully. The feeling should show **through** his control, never by
bursting it. Restraint on the page mirrors restraint in the man.

## Ian — the man and the battle

### What the book is trying to do (intent)

Ian is **not** a self-deceiver, and this is the single most important thing to get right.
He is not a man who slowly discovers feelings he didn't know he had. The trajectory is
**not** "tension of him realising his feelings." Specifically:

- He is **attracted to Jay from day one**, and he knows it. Leo selects, with great care,
  men Ian could *genuinely* like — cute, authentic, unmanipulative — and Jay is exactly
  that. Ian is not stupid; he sees it immediately.
- He **knows his paranoia is paranoia.** He can't get rid of it and doesn't want to. He
  *fuels* his own suspicion as armor, because he is terrified — the Sam Connelly lesson was
  "I trusted my own feelings and it destroyed me." The suspicion is a chosen defense, not a
  genuine belief that Jay is a threat.
- The variable that grows is **depth, not awareness.** Attraction is the constant from the
  first page; what escalates is the *weight* of feeling, until it grows past what his
  management can neutralize. Even then, he manages it appropriately. **He is a man who
  manages things, and manages them well — with steel resolve.**
- His **fairness is inviolable.** He never lets the suspicion make him unfair. Every stumble
  must be *small and objectively minor*; the scene where he is "harsh" must not be a real
  cruelty. If he ever truly acted badly, he would immediately make amends, because he is at
  bottom a fair and kind man.

So the dramatic tension is **not** "will he admit it?" — he already has, to himself. It is
**"can he keep managing as it deepens, and what does it cost him?"** A quieter, more
sophisticated tension, and a deliberate swerve away from the cliché.

### What's working (keep)

- **The premise is stated on schedule, not front-loaded.** Ch4 S1 ("his attraction to Jay
  was undeniable") at one month is correct — of course he knows by then. The Ch1 beats —
  *moved by Jay's honesty, then immediately suspicious of his own reaction* — are the thesis
  in miniature: feels it, examines the feeling, moves to manage it. That's a man with a
  protocol, not a man discovering anything.
- **The depth-ladder is present and genuinely rising:** attraction (Ch4) → "compelling,
  smother it with work" (Ch7) → involuntary longing in the Christmas dream (Ch8) → "he'd
  become necessary" (Ch12 worry) → "fondness beyond professional respect" (Ch15 margin
  notes) → "heart on a leash" (Ch16) → "how much longer can I keep the charade" (Ch17) →
  "in love, had been for months" (Ch20). Weight increasing, not a loop.
- **Fairness holds.** The one "harsh" scene (Ch4 S2, the Falconridge brush-off) is
  objectively mild — a reasonable professional boundary; the crushing is all Jay's internal
  amplification. And Ian makes amends (the contract apology Ch5 S5; fully the watch Ch20 S5).
  Even his coldest lapse — the fifteen seconds of cold courtesy at the first quarterly party
  (Ch5 S1) — he later *owns* in his own POV (Ch14 S4). The rule is being followed.
- **The wound transmutes; it does not recede.** The suspicion-of-Jay surface resolves, but
  the underlying terror persists — by Arc 4 it simply points at his own heart instead of at
  Jay. Correct shape.
- **Best of all: the fall is externalised in actions and small involuntary breaches**, not
  just narrated. The private PI report (Ch6), burying himself in Hartwell to smother it
  (Ch7), the 3 a.m. call and three hours of arctic tennis (Ch8), the cigarette that breaks a
  years-old promise (Ch17), crossing his own line to read an hour of margin notes (Ch15),
  the birthday text drafted and deleted (Ch20), the watch and apology (Ch20). These are the
  slow burn done right, and none of them makes him unfair or undignified.

### Where it needs work (revision targets)

1. **Cool the few interior lines that break the steel.** A man who manages himself with
   steel resolve should hold that register *even inside his own head* — feeling showing
   *through* the control, not cracking it into purple. A handful of hot lines contradict the
   man (see "Line-level targets" below). This is the strongest, most vision-consistent note:
   depth should read as *pressure under a sealed surface*.

2. **Keep the cost of his management visible.** The risk of a man who manages *well* is that
   the steel reads as "nothing is happening." The antidote is exactly the involuntary
   breaches above, plus his visible solitude and loneliness. Make sure those land — they are
   what convert "controlled" into "controlled at a price."

3. **Prefer the involuntary-tell register over the confess-to-Leo register.** Ian's strongest
   beats are the ones where his control slips *without witness or admission* (the 3 a.m. call,
   the cigarette, fleeing the room, reading the notes, the unsent text). Some of his strain is
   instead *spoken to Leo* ("three days of temptation I've been carefully avoiding," Ch14).
   Leo-as-pressure-valve is good and worth keeping, but dialogue *tells* the struggle while the
   involuntary tells *show* it. Lean toward showing.

4. **Mirror balance in the back half.** Late in the book Ian's wound goes *fully internal*
   (rumination) while Jay's keeps getting *externally triggered* (Max, the gala, the filing
   mistake). For symmetry, give Ian one or two more *external* flares of the old Sam-terror —
   the Ch19 S3 camera-paranoia spike is the perfect model, and it's over in a single sentence.
   Let one or two like it breathe a beat longer. It does **not** make him a self-deceiver: he
   sees the flare for what it is and overrides it, which is more *him*, not less. It simply
   keeps his wound reacting to the world the way Jay's does.

5. **(Minor) A touch more of Ian misjudging himself, in his own POV.** Jay narrates his own
   self-deprecation constantly, so we feel the gap between his self-image (coward, disposable)
   and his actual courage. Ian's parallel false self-image ("barren," incapable of warmth) is
   stated more sparingly and mostly inferred. Two or three more moments of Ian quietly
   *miscounting himself as cold/empty* — and being wrong — would balance the symmetry of the
   "twisted self-image" theme.

## The two mirrors (the central theme)

### Intent

Jay and Ian are mirrors: wary, distrustful, hurt — and each doing his best to act with
dignity. Both are managing *fine* on their own; neither is to read as too strong or too
weak. The crucial twist is **twisted self-image**:

- Jay believes himself a coward and disposable; he is in fact brave, competent, and adored.
- Ian believes himself barren and cold; he is in fact deeply caring and protective.

Both are strong and caring people who cannot see it in themselves.

### Assessment

- **Landing well.** The "Solitude is comfortable." / "On that, we both agree." exchange at
  the retreat (Ch17 S4) is the thesis of the book in two lines. The parallel coping —
  Ian's meal-prep and gym discipline against Jay's batch-cooking and shirt-ironing rituals,
  both retreating into work and chosen solitude — is consistent and well-textured.
- **The one asymmetry to watch:** Jay's twisted self-image is voiced constantly in his own
  register; Ian's is quieter (see Ian work-item #5). The mirror is real but currently
  louder on Jay's side. Balancing Ian's self-misjudgment is the lever.

## Fragile hope, resilience, and the "hostile" world

**Intent:** Book 1's emotional key is fragile hope that keeps swaying, and resilience in
the face of a hostile world.

**Assessment — with one thing to decide consciously:** the *swaying* is the engine and it
works (hope up → crushed by the joke-hire reveal → rebuilt → tested by gala/flu/mistake →
restored). But note the actual texture: **Turner & Associates is a sanctuary, not a hostile
world.** The genuine antagonists (Cruver/Orrey, Max's thoughtlessness, Leo's meddling) are
minor and quickly resolved or redeemed. The hostility lives almost entirely in the
characters' *histories and anticipations* — the threat is hypervigilance meeting a world
that is mostly kind.

That is poignant and may be exactly the book you want (a comfort slow-burn where the wound
is the lens, not the present). Just hold it as a *choice*: the current book is "wounded
people healing in a safe place," more than "resilience under ongoing external pressure." If
you want the hostile-world note to bite harder, it would need a little more present-tense
friction; if not, the internalised version is working.

## Slice-of-life slow burn

**Intent:** mundane daily life, two people growing close day after day until they have
dedicated their lives to each other without noticing when it happened. Book 1 = the two of
them becoming *acquaintances*. It must feel natural.

### What's working

- The closeness genuinely accretes through **work** — the late-evening parallel-productivity
  motif, the recurring "Ian's office light still on," the slow accumulation of shared
  references (the philistine bond at the concert, "bold artistic expression," the
  sleep-talking-about-Rus gag). The "grew close without noticing when" effect is achieved.
- The **seasonal structure** is doing quiet thematic work — each ~3-month arc is a season;
  winter is the low point (isolation, illness, the filing mistake); spring is the thaw. Worth
  leaning into even more deliberately.

### Strain points (be aware, not alarmed)

- **Leo is a plot-machine.** He engineers the hire, the room-share, the concert tickets — a
  lot of the proximity is *manufactured* by a meddler rather than drifting together. It works,
  but the **retreat room-share (Ch14)** is the most device-y beat in an otherwise organic book.
  Decide how much weight you want Leo's hand to carry versus natural drift.
- **The retreat (Ch17) is a burn-rate spike.** After sixteen chapters of slow accretion, three
  days compress a great deal of intimacy. It's the Arc 4 hinge, so *some* acceleration is right
  — just know it's your most *noticeable* closeness-jump, which slightly cuts against "didn't
  notice when it happened."
- **A few beats recur past their value.** The bonus-shock choreography ("stares at the number
  in disbelief") happens ~three times (Ch3 monthly, Ch9 the $80k, Ch19 quarterly) — diminishing
  returns. The lunch-where-do-we-eat debate and "Jay declines Friday drinks" are mostly a
  *strength* (ritual is the genre), but a couple of instances could be varied so they read as
  rhythm, not recycling.

## Line-level targets (for the revision pass)

Candidate "too-hot" interior lines that break Ian's sealed surface — cool or compress so the
pull shows through the control rather than cracking it:

- **Ch8 S5 (Dream):** "the sounds he made drove Ian to madness." Trim toward restraint.
- **Ch12 S2 (Discomfort):** the explicit fantasy paragraph — "scenarios of slowly removing
  those beautiful clothes, discovering the body beneath, learning how his skin might feel and
  how he might respond to being touched." This is the single biggest tonal outlier for a
  steel-resolve man in a mostly-Jay-POV slow burn. Keep the pull; lose the catalogue. (Heat
  level is ultimately your call — flag, not mandate.)
- **Ch12 S1/S2:** "the strongest urge he'd felt in years." Same family; soften.

Stretch to watch for the involuntary-tell balance: **Ch11–Ch14** is mostly smooth, competent
management. It does carry real breaches (the frantic worry in Ch12 S4; the margin notes in
Ch15 S5), but the Ch13–Ch14 middle is the quietest. One more small *involuntary* tell in that
stretch — something he does for Jay, or denies himself about Jay, that he'd be embarrassed to
have noticed — would keep the rising weight visible rather than narrated.

## Jay (to revisit — separate pass)

Jay's progression is largely satisfying: slowly, month by month, a little more confident.
Two threads flagged here for the dedicated Jay pass, not addressed now:

- **How Jay perceives Ian's coldness.** The intended arc: no baseline → reads Ian as merely
  rigid/professional → over time notices he is treated *colder than others* → it begins to
  bother him. This perception curve is a key device for the mirror and deserves a deliberate
  pass to make sure the noticing is paced and earned.
- **Career progression.** How Jay's role and competence visibly grow still has work; worth a
  focused look at pacing and the markers of his advancement.

## Continuity cross-reference: the July 14 birthday

Captured here because it touches a structural constraint. The book wants three things that
**cannot all be true in any single year**: Jay's first day on **Monday, Aug 1**; the probation
review on **Monday, Oct 31**; and Jay's birthday on **Saturday, July 14**. July 14 → Aug 1 is
always 18 days (= +4 weekdays), so a Saturday July 14 *forces* a Wednesday Aug 1 — it can never
be a Monday. The Monday work anchors are load-bearing (stated in dialogue, twice; they drive
the probation structure); the Saturday birthday is one line of narration. Cheapest
reconciliation: bend the birthday weekday — change "July 14th fell on a Saturday this year" to
just "July 14th," or to "a Friday." (Logged as flag #15 in the timeline.)
