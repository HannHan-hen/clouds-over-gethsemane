# Book 2 — Craft Notes: Intent, Execution, and Revision Targets

A working record of *what Book 2 is trying to do*, how well it currently does it, and
the specific places to tighten. Book 1's craft notes led with Ian (his recognition arc
was that book's engine); Book 2 leads with **Jay**, because the second year is *his*
recognition arc — the symmetrical answer to the first.

This is a craft/intent companion, not a continuity log. For dating issues see
`timeline/book2-timeline.md` (its "drift & flags" section); read it alongside this — the
spring/summer-2018 calendar drift is the one structural problem flagged for the editing pass.

Read `guidelines/book1-craft-notes.md` first. Much of Book 2's machinery is Book 1's,
one turn deeper, and several revision targets are the *same* targets escalated.

---

## Foundation: where Book 2 sits, and the doubled irony

**The series shape.** Book 1 was **Ian's** recognition arc — attraction from page one,
managed with steel, deepening until he privately names it love at the very end. **Book 2
is the mirror: Jay's** recognition arc — affection he files as "admiration/gratitude,"
deepening under denial until he privately names it love at the very end (Ch39 S3,
the gala terrace: "in love with this man. Deeply, completely, hopelessly"). The two books
rhyme on purpose. Jay walks, one book behind, the exact road Ian walked: feel it → reframe
it as something safe and lesser → have the reframing fail. **Protect that symmetry** — it
is the cleanest structural idea in the series, and it means Jay's ladder should be drawn
with the same care Book 1 gave Ian's.

If Book 1 was "two people becoming acquaintances," **Book 2 is "two people building a
shared life without admitting what it is"** — domestic intimacy, blended worlds, reciprocal
care — while the confession is deliberately withheld. Book 3 then strains the dam.

**Narration.** Same omniscient register, still anchored mostly to Jay, with Ian's interior
fully visible. But the **dramatic irony is now doubled**: in Book 1 the reader was in on
*one* secret Jay couldn't see (Ian's battle). In Book 2 the reader is in on *two* — both
men are in love, and **each misreads the other's love as a lesser feeling** (Jay reads
Ian's devotion as noble mentorship; Ian reads Jay's devotion as a good assistant's
gratitude). The engine is no longer "will he admit it to himself?" for either of them
individually; it is **"two people certain of their own love and certain it isn't returned,
managing the ache in parallel."**

**The tightrope (carried over and sharpened).** Both leads must read as *intelligent people
in denial*, not as *oblivious people who can't see the obvious*. The difference is active
vs. passive: denial that the reader watches them *perform on themselves* is poignant;
blindness that just persists is frustrating. This is the single biggest risk Book 2 runs,
and most of the revision targets below are facets of it.

## Jay — the recognition arc (the spine of the book)

### What the book is trying to do (intent)

- Jay enters Book 2 calling his feeling **admiration** for the best man he's ever known. He
  is not lying; he genuinely lacks the vocabulary, having spent his life with "other
  priorities" (Ch21, the "ace"/never-dated self-understanding shared with Zoe). The arc is
  the slow, frightened acquisition of the right word.
- The growth variable is **the same as Book 1 Ian's: depth, not awareness** — until late.
  For most of the book Jay isn't hiding a known love; he's mislabeling a real one. The
  turn comes when circumstances make the label undeniable.
- **The crisis is the proof.** The job-search spiral (Ch31–33) is engineered to strip
  everything else away — the apartment, the savings, the job itself — so that the *one*
  thing Jay discovers he cannot give up is **Ian**. That's the lever that converts
  "admiration/job-security" into "I don't want to live without him" (Ch32 S5). The romance
  is revealed *through* the near-loss.
- **His self-image still blocks him**, now romantically: the Book 1 "I'm disposable / out of
  place" wound becomes "someone like me could never matter to someone like Ian *that way*"
  (the impossibility is stated as obvious, repeatedly). Twisted self-image is once again the
  thing standing between him and the truth.
- **He seeks shelter, then outgrows the shelter.** Book 1's safe tiny studio was the
  refuge. Book 2's arc is physical as well as emotional: he leaves the studio for a home in
  Ian's world, twelve minutes from Ian's door (Ch35). The move *is* the arc — hiding-in-a-
  shelter becoming building-a-life — and the studio-grief (Ch38 S2) is its honest cost.

### The depth-ladder (it's present and rising — keep it legible)

admiration / "the best person I've ever known" (Ch21) → Leo plants the seed, *what if he
feels something* (Ch22 S3) → an "anchor," someone who *matters* (Ch25 S1) → physical
awareness he files under "gay panic, everyone has it about Ian" (Ch24 dream; Ch30 "you
looked beautiful") → the mutual-trauma hug, "I would rather die than cause him harm"
(Ch27) → the melancholy after the ferry day, grief at a closeness he thinks is illusory
(Ch28) → the crisis: the thing he can't lose is Ian (Ch32–33) → Fay names it: "head over
heels in love" (Ch32 S4) → flat denial, "definitely not love" (Ch32 S5) → "I don't want to
live without him" (Ch32 S5) → the terrace, named at last: "hopelessly in love" (Ch39 S3).
Weight rising, not a loop — the same clean shape Book 1 drew for Ian. The job is to keep
each rung *felt*, not just logged.

### What's working (keep)

- **The crisis-as-proof structure is excellent and is the book's best idea after the
  symmetry.** Ch31–33 makes the romance legible by threatening to remove it. Jay can
  rationalize staying for money, safety, the apartment — and the spiral takes all three off
  the table, leaving only Ian. "I could survive without the money and the status... but
  having to live and never see Ian again felt devastating" (Ch32 S4) is the thesis sentence
  of Jay's arc.
- **"Shows up regardless" still holds**, and matures into a decision: the whole back third
  is Jay choosing, consciously, to stay and *endure* the stress for Ian's sake ("If that was
  the price for staying with Ian, he'd pay it," Ch32 S5). Book 1's reflex becomes Book 2's
  resolve.
- **The reciprocal-care inversion is a strong, early signal.** Book 1 was Ian caring for
  Jay; Book 2 opens (Ch21 S1) with *Jay* monitoring *Ian's* skipped lunch, then his eating
  and secret smoking (Ch31, Ch33). The caretaking becomes mutual, which is exactly how
  "boss/assistant" quietly converts to "partners."
- **The outside confirmations land** (Fay, Rus, Mic/Van, Leo) — the people around Jay see it
  before he does, which both advances the recognition and keeps it from being purely
  internal monologue. Fay's diagnosis (Ch32) is the cleanest.

### Where it needs work (revision targets)

1. **Damp the amplitude of the spiral — this is the same note as Book 1 Jay #1, escalated.**
   The Ch31–33 crash is the *third* full Jay-collapse of the series (after Cruver and the
   filing mistake in Book 1), and it bottoms out harder than ever — dropped table → lost
   savings → cancel the apartment → quit-and-flee-to-a-gas-station fantasy. Some depth is
   right (the stakes are now romantic, which earns escalation). But the crash still
   *re-opens the Arc-1 core wound at near-full intensity* ("the joke hire... I don't belong
   here," Ch31 S5) — the same bottom Jay hit two years and one book ago. For a maturing
   character, this late valley should **sting without fully re-litigating "I am worthless."**
   Consider letting the wound this time be specifically *romantic* ("I can't be near him and
   not have him") rather than a regression to the original belonging-wound.

2. **Make the climb out more self-driven (also a repeat of Book 1 Jay #2).** The recovery is
   once again largely *administered*: Fay names the feeling, the gym crew diagnoses it, Rus
   pushes, and the decisive unsticking is Ian's retention package (Ch33) — the contract is
   handed to Jay, and the offer he declines is the universe's doing as much as his. Jay does
   reach his own resolve ("I can't leave him," Ch32 S5), which is good — but let *that*
   internal decision, not the new contract, be the turn. Right now the order reads
   slightly as "Jay decides to stay → and is conveniently rewarded," when "Jay decides to
   stay *despite* believing he'll keep suffering" is the braver, more earned version the
   text is reaching for.

3. **Let competence-growth show in discrete beats (carried from Book 1 Jay #3 and career
   thread).** Jay is now a coordinator co-interviewing hires, training juniors, running the
   New Continent trip's logistics, surviving strategy season — real scope growth. But much
   of it is *told* ("he handled it well," "months of experience had seasoned him"). The
   interview week (Ch36) is the best discrete proof — he reads character better than Ian
   does and Ian defers to him. Find two or three more moments like that, where Jay visibly
   does something the Book-1 Jay couldn't, and let one or two *register* on him before he
   deflects.

### Thread: the sexuality recognition (spans into Book 3 — handle as one arc)

Book 2 opens the question (Ch21 S5: Jay, gently, accepts Zoe's "ace" and "you're one of us
queers" — never having dated by choice, a college failure he won't discuss) and ends with
him in love with a man (Ch39) **without ever reconciling the two.** That gap is deliberate
and fine, but it means the label-processing ("at least bisexual," the fear of physical
intimacy) lands in **Book 3 (Ch41 S4)**, not here. Track it as a single cross-book thread so
the Book 2 "ace/not-interested" framing and the Book 3 "in love with a man, what does that
make me" don't read as a contradiction the text forgot about — they're a setup and a payoff
a book apart.

## Ian — from smothering the feeling to engineering around it

### What the book is trying to do (intent)

Book 1's Ian *managed* the feeling (smothered it with work, kept it sealed). **Book 2's Ian
has surrendered to it internally** — "done pretending that spending time with Jay wasn't the
driving force behind most of his decisions" (Ch26 S1) — and now **acts on it, covertly.**
The new register is a man loving "from the shadows" who will cross small ethical and
professional lines to keep Jay close, *knows* he is doing it, feels the guilt, and cannot
stop. This is a genuine evolution, and a slightly darker, richer Ian:

- He **manufactures proximity**: invents the New Continent business trip to soothe Jay's
  travel fear and claim him for several days (Ch22); bribes the agent to settle Jay into a
  flat twelve minutes away and arranges the impossible mortgage and the bank (Ch29, Ch30);
  recruits Ray specifically to lighten the workload and *reclaim their evenings* (Ch36 S5);
  institutes the nightly drives home (Ch38).
- His **fairness is still inviolable toward others**, but he now bends rules in his *own*
  favor where Jay is concerned — and the book is honest that this is manipulation ("It was
  manipulative, he knew that... his need for Jay was stronger than his discomfort with his
  methods," Ch29 S2).
- The Sam-wound **transmutes again**: Book 1's terror pointed at Jay, then at his own heart;
  Book 2's points at *the world that could take Jay away* (jealousy of Zoe, Liv, Gal, Eli;
  the conviction that someone "appropriate" will claim Jay and he has no right to stop it).

### What's working (keep)

- **The engineering is externalised in actions**, exactly the register Book 1's notes
  praised — the slow burn shown, not narrated. The trip, the apartment, the bank, the Ray
  recruitment, the drives: these are love made of *deeds*, and each is shadowed by his
  own guilt, which keeps him sympathetic.
- **The guilt and restraint are foregrounded**, which is what keeps "controlling" from
  curdling into "creepy." He repeatedly checks himself ("I should be strong enough to send
  Jay away for his own good. But he wasn't," Ch42-adjacent register; the recurring "this is
  selfish and I'll do it anyway").
- **The jealousy is rendered as small and self-aware**, not possessive entitlement — "petty
  and insistent, making him feel small and selfish and utterly pathetic" (Ch42-register;
  cf. Ch38 S3 at the party). He never acts on it against the rivals; he just suffers it.

### Where it needs work (revision targets)

1. **Install a clear guard-rail on the engineering so it stays "loving and pathetic," never
   "controlling."** Collectively, Ian *arranges Jay's life without consent* — his job
   security, his apartment, his mortgage, his evenings, his coworker (Ray). Individually each
   is sweet; in aggregate it can tip. Two protections, both already partly present — keep
   them load-bearing: **(a)** Jay's agency must stay visible at every hinge (he chooses the
   flat, he sets the terms, he declines/accepts on his own reasoning), and **(b)** Ian's
   guilt must *cost* him, not just be noted. The strongest version is Ch29's half-confession
   to Jay about the agent — Ian voluntarily surrendering some of the control he engineered.
   More beats like that (Ian *undoing* or *owning* his own scheme) inoculate the pattern.

2. **Give Ian's Sam-terror an external flare during the Gal crisis (the exact Book 1 #4
   note).** Gal's false accusation (Ch40) *literally re-runs the thing that destroyed Ian* —
   a trusted-seeming subordinate weaponizing a false claim — yet Ian's own old terror is
   mostly **reported afterward by Leo** (Ch40 S4) rather than *felt by Ian in the moment*.
   Let the parallel land on Ian on the page: a flare of the Sam-dread when the complaint
   hits, seen-for-what-it-is and overridden (which is more *him*, not less). This both
   deepens Gal's function as a mirror and balances the wound-reactivity between the leads.

3. **Watch the "both pine in parallel" coda — it's Ian's half of the book's most over-used
   beat (see Beat-level targets).** Ian's chapters disproportionately *end* on the same
   interior note: he sits alone, twelve minutes / a wall away, wanting what he can't have.
   It's true to him and sometimes gorgeous (Ch28 S5; Ch31 S5), but it recurs near-identically
   and dilutes. Vary the cadence; let some chapters close on a *deed* or a wry beat instead
   of the ache.

## The two mirrors, deepened (the central theme)

### Intent

Book 1's mirror: two wounded people, both managing with dignity, both with a **twisted
self-image** (Jay thinks himself a coward; Ian thinks himself barren). Book 2 deepens it
into the romance's actual obstacle:

- **Both are now in love, and both believe it can't be returned** — Jay because he's "out
  of Ian's league," Ian because he's undeserving and certain to be left for someone
  "appropriate."
- **Each misreads the other's love as a lesser feeling** (mentorship ↔ gratitude). The very
  thing that would free them — *the other one feels it too* — is the one fact each is most
  certain is false.

The twisted self-image has graduated from *background characterization* to *the engine of
the held confession*: neither can confess because neither can believe the confession would
be welcome.

### Assessment

- **Landing, and structurally elegant.** The Ch27 trauma-exchange is the thesis scene — each
  confesses his deepest wound (Ian's disownment and poverty; Jay's harassment and his
  mother's protection) and each tells the *other* "it was not your fault," while remaining
  unable to extend that absolution to himself. The mutual-absolution / no-self-absolution
  pattern is the Book 1 mirror perfected into dialogue.
- **The asymmetry to watch is the inverse of Book 1's.** In Book 1 Jay's self-image was
  voiced loudly and Ian's quietly. In Book 2 the *mutual misreading* is voiced almost
  *equally and constantly* — which creates the opposite risk: not imbalance but **repetition**.
  The "Jay thinks Ian only feels X / Ian thinks Jay only feels Y" beat is stated so often it
  can make both leads read as obtuse. Convert some statements of it into **active
  near-realizations they talk themselves out of** (see the held-confession note).

## The held confession (the tension that must NOT resolve — hold it as a choice)

This is Book 2's equivalent of Book 1's candid "the hostile world is actually a sanctuary"
note: the thing to look at squarely rather than let run on autopilot.

**Intent:** the slow burn's whole engine is that they get *closer and closer* — Christmas
alone together, the ferry "date," the hug, "you looked beautiful," the retreat, the
nightly drives, a spare key — and still **neither names it.** The pleasure is in the
withholding; the cost of the withholding is the drama.

**The risk:** by the back half, the leads have done nearly everything but say the word, and
the reasons for silence have to keep *working* or the hold reads as authorial stalling
rather than character truth. The credible blocks are all present — the **power imbalance**
(boss/assistant), **Ian's trust-trauma** (Sam: "the last time I trusted my feelings it
destroyed me"), and **Jay's unworthiness belief** (out of his league). The note is to make
sure **every near-confession is blocked by one of those, specifically and freshly**, not by
a timing accident or a convenient interruption.

**The lever (and the fix for the repetition problem above):** prefer **active denial over
passive blindness.** The strongest beats are the ones where a lead *almost* sees it and
*chooses* to disbelieve — Jay deciding the hug "meant nothing romantic," Ian deciding Jay's
care is "the devotion of a good assistant." Those read as intelligent people protecting
themselves. The weaker beats are the flat assertions ("of course Ian couldn't feel that
way"). Tilt the ratio toward the former, and the whole "neither names it" machine stops
feeling like obtuseness and starts feeling like two frightened people each guarding the
other's exit.

## Shared trauma and revealed pasts (intimacy through vulnerability)

**Intent:** the closeness in Book 2 is purchased largely with *disclosure* — each lowers the
guard the other has never seen lowered. This is the "deepening intimacy through shared
trauma" the series bible names.

**What's working:**

- **Ch27 is the keystone** and it's excellent: the blind-date rescue cracks open Ian's
  never-told history (disowned with nothing, sold his watch, eight years in a studio worse
  than Jay's), which in turn unlocks Jay's (the first-job assault, his mother's "he's a pig,
  it wasn't your fault"). The exchange *earns* a quantum of intimacy that the rest of the
  book then spends.
- **The disclosures are reciprocal and load-bearing**, not decorative — each reframes the
  other's present (Ian's "I'm a pariah too," Ch24, dismantles Jay's class shame; Jay's
  mother-story gives Ian a model of the absolution his own family withheld).

**Watch:** disclosure is potent precisely because it's rare — don't let it become the
*default* mode of connection. Ch24, Ch27, and the retreat (Ch37) each open a vein; that's
about right for a 20-chapter book. If later chapters reach for another big reveal, prefer
instead the *quiet, undramatic* intimacy (cooking, the drives, the duck-feeding) — the
slice-of-life register is the book's other, gentler engine and shouldn't be crowded out by
serial confession.

## Domestic slow-burn — the new texture (off-the-clock togetherness)

**Intent:** Book 1's closeness accreted through *work* (the parallel-productivity late
evenings). Book 2's must migrate **off the clock** — chosen, private, domestic time — because
that's what converts colleagues into something neither will name.

**What's working:**

- **The new rituals are well-built and genuinely off-clock:** the ferry/garden/duck-feeding
  "date" (Ch28), Christmas dinner cooked together at Ian's (Ch30), the retreat's shared scent
  and absinthe (Ch37), the nightly drives, the spare key (Ch35). These are the migration done
  right — the relationship now lives in spaces that have nothing to do with Turner &
  Associates.
- **The "is it a date?" denial comedy is a reliable, on-theme engine** (Ch28's "Not a date.
  Just an outing"; the matching aprons; Rus and Fay both reading it correctly). It lets the
  reader enjoy the romance the leads won't admit to.
- **The apartment-as-metaphor is strong** — moving from the studio-shelter into a home in
  Ian's district physically enacts Jay's arc, and the housewarming (Ch35) where gym friends,
  coworkers, Ian, and Leo all mix in his living room is the visual thesis of "his two worlds
  have blended."

**Strain points (be aware, not alarmed):**

- **Leo is still the plot-machine** (Book 1 note carried forward) — he engineers the styling,
  is the conduit for the apartment scheme, drives the birthday-party seed. As in Book 1 it
  works, but a lot of the proximity is still *manufactured by a meddler*. Ian's own
  engineering (above) now *doubles* the manufactured-proximity quotient; between the two of
  them, relatively little of the closeness "just drifts." Decide consciously how much hand
  you want on the scale.
- **The retreat (Ch37) is again a burn-rate spike**, exactly as Ch17 was in Book 1 — three
  storm-bound days compress a great deal (the shared scent, the absinthe night, waking
  tangled). It's an arc hinge so *some* acceleration is right; just know it's the book's most
  *noticeable* intimacy-jump, slightly against the "grew close without noticing when."

## Class, belonging, and the impostor wound (maturing — keep damping it)

**Intent:** Jay's "I don't belong in Ian's world" wound persists but should **shrink** across
the book as he matures, the way Book 1 wanted his crash-depth to shrink.

**Assessment:**

- **The progression is there.** The tailor humiliations (Ch22–23) reopen the class wound at
  full force; Ian's "I'm a pariah too" (Ch24) reframes it; the business trip and the move
  test and gradually *settle* it, until the housewarming shows his worlds coexisting without
  shame.
- **But the impostor interiority recurs near full-strength later than it should.** The "I'm
  out of place / he's out of my league / I'm just visiting his world" register keeps
  returning at near-original intensity (the gala-impostor feeling, the "we are fundamentally
  different worlds" close of Ch22, and onward). As with Book 1 Jay #1, **let the later
  instances be shallower** — a flinch rather than a spiral — so the maturation is legible.
  The tailor beat in particular has now run in both books; if it recurs again it needs a
  *new* turn, not a re-statement.

## Gal — the present-tense antagonist, and the Sam-mirror

**Intent / what she fixes.** Book 1's craft notes flagged that its "hostile world" was
really a sanctuary — the antagonists minor and quickly resolved, the threat mostly internal.
**Gal is Book 2's correction:** a genuine, present-tense, escalating threat inside the safe
place (Ch38–40). She gives the back third real external friction the first book lacked.

**Her best, underused idea — the double mirror:**

- **Gal is the dark mirror of Jay** — the new hire who fixates, the one whose neediness reads
  as harmless until it doesn't. Jay even half-recognizes himself in her ("Was I ever as
  dramatic as Gal?", Ch41-register / cf. his sympathy in Ch39), which is a lovely,
  uncomfortable beat: he extends to her the benefit of the doubt he was once given, and it's
  weaponized against him.
- **Gal re-runs Ian's Sam** — a trusted-seeming subordinate who deploys a **false accusation**
  (Ch40). This is the richest thing about her, and it's currently *stated* (Leo spells out the
  parallel after the fact, Ch40 S4) more than *felt* (see Ian revision #2).

**Where it needs work:**

1. **Cash the Sam-parallel on the page during the crisis, through Ian's reaction** (see Ian
   #2). Right now Gal's plot resolves cleanly and quickly (cameras exonerate Jay; she's fired
   same-day) — which is *narratively* satisfying but *thematically* leaves the Ian-Sam echo
   on the table. The fast resolution is fine; the missed beat is Ian's interior.
2. **She arrives late and resolves fast.** As the book's one real antagonist she carries a
   lot of structural weight in only three chapters. That's acceptable (the book isn't about
   her), but it means the "false accusation" — a heavy, real-world-loaded device — gets less
   room to breathe than its weight implies. Make sure Jay's *recovery* from it (Ch40 S5 → the
   resolve to stay anyway) is given enough beats that the accusation doesn't read as a plot
   speed-bump.

## Leo — meddler turned truth-teller

**Intent/role evolution.** In Book 1 Leo manufactured proximity (the hire, the room-share,
the concert). In Book 2 his function widens: he's still the plot-machine (styling, the
party seed), but he becomes **the one who keeps naming what neither lead will say** — "you're
completely gone over him," "painfully, devastatingly sweet," the explicit "you adore him."
He's the reader's proxy, voicing the obvious.

**Assessment:** this works and is often the comic relief valve the tension needs. **Watch
two things:** (1) Leo is now doing *narrative* work for both Book 2's proximity-engineering
*and* its theme-articulation, which is a lot of load on one device — make sure the leads
also reach insights without Leo handing them over; and (2) Leo's truth-telling can do the
job the leads' *active near-realizations* should be doing (see held-confession note). Let
Leo confirm what Jay and Ian have *almost* worked out, rather than supply it cold.

## Beat-level / line-level recurrence targets (for the revision pass)

Book 2's rituals are mostly a *strength* (ritual is the genre). These are the few that have
crossed from rhythm into recycling:

- **The "both pine in parallel" chapter-coda.** The book's signature closing move — *Jay
  thinks Ian only feels X; Ian thinks Jay only feels Y; twelve minutes / a wall apart, both
  lie awake wanting.* It is sometimes the best writing in the book (Ch28 S5; Ch31 S5) and it
  recurs **near-identically across most chapters**, often verbatim in structure ("twelve
  minutes away," "still in love and still silent"). This is the single clearest line-level
  target: **keep three or four of the strongest, vary or cut the rest**, and let some
  chapters close on a deed, a joke, or a forward beat instead of the ache.
- **The bonus-shock choreography** (carried from Book 1's note) recurs again — the year-end
  bonus (Ch30) and the 80k paycheck (Ch34) both get the "stared at the number, refreshed it,
  it didn't change" treatment. Diminishing returns; vary or compress.
- **The drunk-Jay / Ian-takes-care-of-him beat** (Ch27, and explicitly named as a recurring
  pattern by Book 3) — lovely once, but it's becoming a fixed mechanism. When it recurs,
  give it a *new* function each time (Ch27's is disclosure; make any repeat earn its place).
- **The dinner → drive home → linger in the car coda.** The off-clock rituals are on-theme,
  but the *specific* "Ian pays over Jay's protest; they linger; 'see you Monday'" sequence
  repeats often enough to blur. Vary the texture of a few.
- **"Out of his league / different worlds" as a closing thought** (Ch22 S5 and onward) — same
  family as Book 1's impostor note; damp the later instances.

## Continuity cross-reference: the calendar (the one structural editing problem)

Captured here because it will need a deliberate decision in the editing pass, not a quiet
fix. Book 2's autumn-and-winter dating is solid (it locks to the real 2016/17→2017/18
calendar: the New Continent trip, Christmas, New Year, the January gala all fall on the
correct weekdays). **Spring and summer 2018 drift**, and two holidays in particular need
reconciling — these are logged in full in `timeline/book2-timeline.md` (flags #7–8 and the
"Calendar reconciliation — EDITING PRIORITY" callout):

- **Easter** is placed in *mid-April* (after the Q1 reports, with the move following), but the
  real 2018 Easter was **April 1** — too early for the Q1-reports-then-Easter chain the
  chapters depend on. Decide consciously: pull Q1 reports earlier to clear an April-1 Easter,
  or accept a deliberately fictional late Easter — and hold it consistent against Book 1
  (which honored the real Easter).
- **Jay's 31st birthday** is narrated as a **Sunday**, July 14; July 14, 2018 is a **Saturday**
  (and the family-visit "Friday the nineteenth" is actually a Thursday). The date 14 July is
  canonical; bend the weekday words.

Neither is a theme problem, but both sit on *structural* anchors (reporting seasons, the
family-visit weekend, the birthday party), so they're the calendar items most worth fixing
before a revision pass hardens the surrounding scenes.
